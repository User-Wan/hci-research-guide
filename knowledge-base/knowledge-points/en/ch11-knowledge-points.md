# Chapter 11: Analyzing Qualitative Data — Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

**Qualitative Data Analysis** is the process of turning unstructured data found in texts, multimedia, and other artifacts into detailed descriptions about important aspects of the situation or problem under consideration. Unlike quantitative analysis that deals with numerical measures, qualitative analysis involves human coding of ambiguous textual and multimedia content, making it more susceptible to biased interpretation or subjective manipulation.

**Content Analysis** is a systematic, replicable technique for compressing many words of text into fewer content categories based on explicit rules of coding. A broader definition allows for other types of information, defining it as "any technique for making inferences by objectively and systematically identifying specified characteristics of messages." Content analysis applies to textual information as well as multimedia materials such as drawings, music, and videos.

**Coding** is the process of assigning categories and descriptors to blocks of text or other qualitative data. It involves interacting with data, making comparisons between data, and deriving concepts to stand for those data, then developing those concepts in terms of their properties and dimensions. Coding is much more than paraphrasing or counting key words.

**Grounded Theory** is a qualitative research method first proposed by Glaser and Strauss that seeks to develop theory that is "grounded in data systematically gathered and analyzed." It is an inductive research method that starts from empirical observations or data and aims to develop well-grounded theories, contrasting with experimental research that starts from preformed hypotheses.

**A Priori Coding** involves using an established theory or hypothesis to guide the selection of coding categories. Categories come from previously published work or from the researcher's own prior investigations. This approach is simpler to use but may sacrifice broader insight.

**Emergent Coding** refers to qualitative analysis conducted without any theory or model to guide the analysis. The researcher starts by noting interesting concepts or ideas and continually refines those ideas until a coherent model emerges. This is the foundation of grounded theory.

**In Vivo Code** is a coding category generated by borrowing terms directly from participants' own words when they describe instances or key elements vividly or accurately. This approach helps ensure that concepts stay as close as possible to the participants' own language.

**Researcher-Denoted Concepts** are coding terms created by the researcher when the original text does not contain a key term to describe the instance of interest. These are contrasted with in vivo codes that come directly from participants.

**Open Coding** is the first stage of grounded theory where the text is analyzed to identify any interesting phenomena. Each unique phenomenon is given a distinctive name or code. The researcher is open to all possibilities that reside in the data without being constrained by preestablished theories.

**Axial Coding** is the process of identifying and defining relationships between concepts and categories that have been identified through open coding. It is a key step in grouping codes into higher-level concepts and categories.

**Selective Coding** occurs when previously coded data is revisited from the context of the emerging theory. This allows for refinement of the theory and further iteration of open and axial codes.

**Codebook (Nomenclature)** is a list of numbered categories intended to represent the full array of possible responses to a specific question. It is organized into a hierarchical structure containing multiple levels with increasing detail.

**Triangulation** in qualitative analysis refers to using multiple data sources to support an interpretation. The data sources may be different instances of the same type of data (multiple participants) or completely different sources (observation and time diaries).

**Validity** in qualitative research means using well-established and well-documented procedures to increase the accuracy of findings. It examines the degree to which an instrument measures what it is intended to measure.

**Reliability** refers to the consistency of results: if different researchers working on a common data set come to similar conclusions, those conclusions are said to be reliable.

**Cohen's Kappa** is a statistical measure that rates interrater reliability on a scale from 0 to 1, accounting for chance agreement between coders. A value of 0 means agreement is completely by chance; 1 means perfect reliability. Values above 0.60 indicate satisfactory reliability.

**Intercoder Reliability** examines whether different coders code the same data in a consistent way. It is measured by calculating agreement between multiple independent coders.

**Intracoder Reliability (Stability)** examines whether the same coder rates data consistently throughout the coding process. If a coder is asked to code the same data multiple times, the coding should be consistent.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

**Inductive vs. Deductive Analysis Paradigm**: Qualitative data analysis, especially grounded theory, operates inductively—starting from data to develop theories. This contrasts with experimental research that starts from hypotheses and deductively tests them. The inductive approach allows theories to emerge from the data through multiple rounds of collection and analysis, producing what some call "reverse-engineered hypotheses."

**Three-Stage Qualitative Analysis Model (Corbin and Strausss)**: Stage 1 identifies major themes and ideas from the data set. Stage 2 drills down into each component to find descriptive properties and dimensions and understand their interrelationships. Stage 3 uses knowledge from studying individual components to make inferences about the original substance. This model emphasizes iterative movement between stages.

**Content Analysis Framework**: Content analysis operates on two categories of content: media content (publications, broadcasts, websites, recordings) and audience content (feedback collected through surveys, interviews, focus groups, diaries, observations). Both text-based and multimedia-based information are analyzed using systematic coding procedures with explicit rules.

**Coding Approaches Continuum**: A spectrum from purely emergent coding (grounded theory) to purely a priori coding (theoretical frameworks). Many studies use a mixture of both. Emergent coding provides broader insight but is more complex; a priori coding is simpler but may miss unexpected patterns. The choice depends on the maturity of the research domain and the availability of established theories.

**Validity Framework**: Three primary approaches to validity—face validity (subjective content assessment), criterion validity (predicting validated measures), and construct validity (testing theoretical constructs through factor analysis). In HCI, validity is established through well-documented databases, chains of evidence, data source triangulation, and consideration of alternative explanations.

**Reliability Framework**: Reliability spans two dimensions—intracoder reliability (stability: same coder, consistent over time) and intercoder reliability (reproducibility: different coders, consistent coding). Measures include percentage agreement (simple but doesn't account for chance) and Cohen's Kappa (accounts for chance agreement). The ultimate goal is ensuring different people code the same text the same way.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Prepare for Analysis

Before starting analysis, define the data set clearly and remove any data that does not meet the criteria. Study the data closely to understand the population from which it is drawn. Understand the specific context of the data—analyze data out of context is meaningless and highly biased. Consider factors like age, gender, profession, education, and domain experience when defining the population.

### Step 2: Initial Reading and Immersion

Read the text from beginning to end before starting any coding. During this first round, resist the urge to write annotations or codes. The purpose is to immerse yourself in the life and experience of participants and get a general, unbiased idea of the data set before focusing on any specific aspects.

### Step 3: Select Coding Approach

Choose between emergent coding (grounded theory), a priori coding (theoretical frameworks), or a mixture:
- For new topics with limited literature: use emergent coding based on grounded theory
- For topics with established theories: use a priori coding to guide category selection
- For complex studies: combine both approaches as appropriate

### Step 4: Conduct Open Coding

Analyze the text to identify interesting phenomena. Look for specific types of statements: objectives, actions, outcomes, consequences, causes, contexts, and strategies. For each phenomenon, either use the participant's own words (in vivo codes) or create researcher-denoted concepts. During coding:
- Look for key items likely to carry valuable information
- Ask questions constantly about the data (sensitizing and theoretical questions)
- Make comparisons at various levels (between categories, between groups, with literature)

### Step 5: Develop Concepts and Categories

Group collections of codes describing similar contents together to form higher-level "concepts," then group concepts into "categories." Construct definitions of concepts and categories. Identify and define relationships between concepts through axial coding. Build a hierarchical codebook structure with multiple levels of increasing detail.

### Step 6: Formulate Theory

Create inferential and predictive statements about the phenomena in the data. Develop explicit causal connections or correlations between concepts and categories. Conduct selective coding by revisiting previously coded data from the context of the emerging theory. Iterate between data collection and analysis to refine the theory.

### Step 7: Ensure Validity

Build a database containing all collected materials and analysis products. Establish chains of evidence linking analytic results back to raw data. Use data source triangulation with multiple independent pieces of data pointing in a common direction. Develop and consider alternative explanations. Give every data point appropriate attention and scrutiny.

### Step 8: Ensure Reliability

Develop explicit coding instructions at the beginning. Train all coders to fully understand the instructions. Test code some data and calculate reliability measures. If desired reliability is not achieved, retrain, discuss disagreements, and revise codebook. Conduct frequent reliability checks throughout formal coding. Calculate Cohen's Kappa—values above 0.60 indicate satisfactory reliability.

### Step 9: Analyze Multimedia Content

Apply the same guidelines used for text content. Identify key instances to describe or annotate. Consider using partially automated approaches: manually annotate a subset, use it to train automated systems, then automatically annotate the rest. Evaluate reliability of annotations. For large data sets, select subsets for manual analysis due to high labor costs.

## 4. Decision Criteria

**When to Use Qualitative Analysis**: Use when the research involves texts, observations, video, and artifacts to understand complex situations. Appropriate for data from ethnography, case studies, interviews, focus groups, and surveys where numerical measurement is not possible or not the goal.

**Emergent vs. A Priori Coding**: Choose emergent coding when working on new topics with limited literature and no established theories. Choose a priori coding when well-established theories or frameworks exist that can guide category selection. Use a mixture when some aspects have theoretical foundations while others are exploratory.

**Choosing Coders**: Subjective coders (study designers) have domain expertise and need minimal training but may have inflated reliability and constrained thinking. Objective coders are more open to new patterns and less biased but need substantial training and may lack domain knowledge. In practice, subjective coders are commonly used and acceptable when appropriate procedures are followed.

**Content Analysis Scope**: Define clearly what constitutes the data set—consider whether to include public messages, private messages, or both. Restrict the population to those represented in the data. Consider the time period for data inclusion. Analyze data in context—different contexts require different interpretations.

**Text vs. Multimedia Content**: Text content analysis follows established coding procedures. Multimedia analysis is more labor-intensive without automated tools. Use partially automated annotation when possible: manually code a subset, train the system, then automatically annotate the rest. Select subsets for manual analysis of large multimedia data sets.

**Reliability Threshold**: Cohen's Kappa values have well-accepted interpretations: K ≤ 0.20 (poor), 0.20 < K ≤ 0.40 (fair), 0.40 < K ≤ 0.60 (moderate), 0.60 < K ≤ 0.80 (satisfactory), K > 0.80 (near-perfect). Values below 0.60 indicate questionable reliability requiring improvement.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

**Palliative Care IT Support Study**: Kuziemsky and colleagues conducted a grounded theory analysis of 50 hours of interviews with seven professionals (nurses, physicians, counselors), patient charts, and research literature. The study used open and axial coding to form a detailed map of relationships between factors important to palliative care. This exemplifies how grounded theory processes data from multiple sources to build theoretical understanding.

**Children with Down Syndrome Computer Usage Study**: Researchers used a mixture of emergent coding and a priori coding when investigating computer usage behaviors. Most code items at levels three, four, and five were identified through emergent coding, while categories under "Human capabilities" were derived from existing psychology theory. The code structure was built into a hierarchical format with multiple levels of detail. Terms like "curriculum integration" were borrowed directly from parents' responses as in vivo codes.

**Online Community Trust Study Example**: Used to illustrate how data set definition affects analysis. Researchers studying interpersonal trust development must decide whether to include public bulletin board messages, private Instant Messaging exchanges, or both. The choice depends on the research question (community atmosphere impact vs. private communication) and practical access limitations.

**Computer Usage by Children Survey (Exercise)**: An exercise where 50 children aged 8-15 were interviewed. Computer usage objectives were grouped into three categories: educational, communication, and entertainment. Two coders independently coded data with an agreement matrix showing 0.49 agreement on education, 0.11 on communication, and 0.23 on entertainment. This example demonstrates how to calculate Cohen's Kappa and evaluate intercoder reliability.

**Palliative Care Open and Axial Coding Example**: The analysis produced a coding map showing how concepts like "time," "space," and "policy" related to palliative care dimensions. Open codes such as "temporal onset of pain" and "triage and assessment" were identified from raw data, then grouped into axial categories. This illustrates how the iterative coding process constructs understanding of complex phenomena.

**User Burden Scale Study**: Suh and colleagues developed a model for user burden consisting of six constructs and a User Burden Scale. They used both criterion validity (correlation with established measures) and construct validity (factor analysis) to measure the efficacy of the model and scale. This demonstrates how validity testing is applied in practice.

**Automated Multimedia Annotation**: The partially automated approach involves human coders manually annotating a subset of multimedia data, then using the manually coded data to train the application to connect low-level features with high-level concepts. Once a concept detector is established, it can automatically annotate the rest of the data. This is applied to images, video, and audio clips.

**Senior Citizens Computer Difficulties Example**: Used to illustrate how theoretical frameworks guide coding. When studying difficulties older people experience with computers, the well-established human capabilities framework (cognitive, physical, perceptual) provides high-level coding categories. Participant responses are grouped into these three categories, demonstrating how a priori coding uses existing theory.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

**Confusing Coding with Paraphrasing**: A common misunderstanding is that coding is nothing more than paraphrasing text and counting keywords. Coding involves interacting with data, making comparisons, deriving concepts, and developing those concepts in terms of their properties and dimensions. It is a much deeper analytical process.

**Being Overwhelmed During Open Coding**: Novice coders may find it difficult to identify anything interesting in the data, or conversely, may feel the data is so rich that they need to code almost every word. Both extremes are problematic. The solution is to look for specific statement types (objectives, actions, outcomes, consequences, causes, contexts, strategies), ask questions about the data, and make comparisons.

**Starting with Preconceived Theoretical Ideas in Grounded Theory**: Since grounded theory does not start from a preformed concept or hypothesis, researchers must begin without preconceived theoretical ideas so that concepts and theory truly emerge from the data. Preconceived opinions may bias the findings.

**Failing to Define the Data Set Clearly**: Including data that does not meet the definition criteria pollutes the data set and produces biased or misleading results. For example, analyzing private messages when the study focuses on public community interactions, or including data from outside the specified time period.

**Ignoring Context in Data Analysis**: Any words, terms, and claims must be interpreted in the specific context from which they are extracted. Analyzing data without considering context is like comparing apples with pears. Context must be considered at multiple levels—both the higher-level context of the data set and the lower-level context of individual phrases, sentences, or paragraphs.

**Not Using Multiple Coders**: Using a single coder eliminates the possibility of checking reliability. Multiple coders should code the data independently, and reliability measures (Cohen's Kappa) should be calculated. Different coding perspectives strengthen the analysis.

**Cherry-Picking Data**: Researchers may unconsciously stress observations that support their preferred theory while downplaying observations consistent with alternative explanations. Valid qualitative analysis requires giving every data point appropriate attention and considering alternative explanations.

**Ignoring Reliability Checks**: Not calculating or reporting Cohen's Kappa or other reliability measures makes it impossible to evaluate the quality of the coding. Reliability checks should be conducted frequently throughout the coding process, not just at the end.

**Using Percentage Agreement Alone**: Percentage agreement does not account for chance agreement between coders. Two coders may agree on a substantial percentage of cases even when coding randomly. Cohen's Kappa corrects for this by measuring agreement above and beyond chance.

**Not Training Coders Adequately**: Failing to develop explicit coding instructions and properly train all coders leads to inconsistent coding. Coders must fully understand every coding item before beginning. If reliability targets are not met, retraining and codebook revision are necessary.

**Treating Grounded Theory as Purely Linear**: The description of grounded theory (open coding → concepts → categories → theory) implies a linear process, but actual analysis is iterative and non-linear. Identification of new codes through open coding may lead to rereviewing earlier documents, and new relationships between codes may emerge at any stage.

**Over-reliance on Automated Multimedia Analysis**: Completely automated annotation techniques are still highly error-prone due to the semantic gap between low-level features and high-level concepts. While partially automated approaches show promise, fully automated analysis should be used with caution and results should be validated.

**Failing to Document the Chain of Evidence**: Without clear documentation linking analytic results back to raw data, readers cannot verify that conclusions are supported by the evidence. A well-organized database with all materials, procedures, and products of analysis is essential for establishing validity.

**Not Considering Rival Explanations**: Presenting only one interpretation without considering alternatives weakens validity. A discussion showing how a model fits the data better than plausible alternatives is more compelling than presenting a single model in isolation.

**Inflated Reliability from Subjective Coders**: When the same people who developed the coding scheme also conduct the coding, the reported reliability may be inflated due to hidden knowledge about the coding meanings. Mitigation strategies include using objective coders, bias-awareness training, and careful documentation.
