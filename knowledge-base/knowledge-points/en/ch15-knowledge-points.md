# Chapter 15: Working with Human Subjects — Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

**Informed Consent** is the cornerstone of ethical research with human participants. It has two components: "informed" means participants must understand the study's purpose, procedures, potential risks, and how to obtain more information; "consent" means participation must be entirely voluntary and free from coercion. Informed consent documents typically include institution identification, contact information, study purpose, procedures, duration, risks, benefits, alternatives, confidentiality measures, costs, participant rights, and signature.

**Institutional Review Board (IRB)** is a standing committee at universities, hospitals, corporations, and other organizations that reviews and approves research projects involving human subjects. IRBs examine proposed studies for appropriate practices, procedures, goals, and disclosures to protect participants, researchers, and institutions. In the US, IRB policies for government-funded institutions are governed by the Federal Policy for Protection of Human Research Subjects (the Common Rule, 45 CFR part 46).

**Belmont Report** was published in 1979 by the National Commission for the Protection of Human Subjects of Biomedical and Behavioral Research. It established three principles for the treatment of research participants: respect for persons, beneficence, and justice. These principles form the foundation for modern informed consent practices and research ethics.

**Respect for Persons** is a Belmont Report principle that involves allowing individuals to make independent and autonomous decisions regarding their participation in research. Researchers must provide information necessary for decision-making and give special consideration to cases involving illness or disability that may limit autonomous decision-making.

**Beneficence** is a Belmont Report principle referring to the need to minimize possible harm while maximizing possible benefits from research. This principle argues for research that strives to avoid harms to participants while advancing knowledge.

**Justice** is a Belmont Report principle requiring that neither the burdens of participating in research nor the benefits of the research should be limited to certain populations, particularly when some groups may be easily manipulated.

**Deceptive Research** involves studies where participants are not fully informed about the true goals or design of the research. Deception may be necessary when full disclosure would compromise the realism of the study or influence participant behavior. Deceptive studies require careful justification, IRB approval, and typically require debriefing participants afterward.

**Demand Characteristics** is a psychological concept stating that participants in a research study may examine their situation and provide responses that they think will please the researcher. Participants may try to behave in the manner they believe is expected, potentially biasing results.

**Hawthorne Effect** is the hypothesis that the mere act of participating in an experiment will influence user behavior. Although this effect has been debated among scientists, researchers should design studies to minimize its influence by not giving performance feedback and by blinding participants to researcher preferences.

**Domain Expertise** refers to knowledge of the problems, systems, goals, and tools used in a specific line of work. It is largely separable from computer expertise. Participants for HCI studies should have expertise comparable to expected users—both in terms of domain knowledge and computer proficiency.

**Computer Expertise** is a participant characteristic describing their level of experience and skill with computer systems. It is largely separable from domain expertise. The combination of domain and computer expertise determines participant appropriateness for specific studies.

**Confounding Variables** are factors other than the independent variable that could be responsible for observed differences between groups. In multi-group studies, groups should differ only in the relevant attribute while being comparable in all other characteristics to avoid confounds.

**Participant Compensation** refers to financial or material incentives provided to research participants for their time and effort. Compensation should be commensurate with time requested and participant type. Busy professionals may command higher fees than students. Special populations may require creative incentives and accommodations.

**Privacy Protection** involves measures to safeguard participants' personal information and research data. This includes obtaining consent for data collection, limiting information collected, identifying uses, limiting disclosure and retention, securely protecting data, disclosing policies, and providing accountability.

**Common Rule** (45 CFR part 46) is the US Federal Policy for Protection of Human Research Subjects. It describes requirements for institutional review, including categories of studies eligible for expedited review or exemption from review. It applies to government and government-funded institutions.

**A/B Testing** in corporate contexts involves showing different interface versions to different user sets to determine which is preferred. While seemingly low-risk, A/B testing raises questions about informed consent, especially when users are unaware they are in experiments, as illustrated by the Facebook emotional contagion study.

**Power Analysis** is a statistical technique used to determine the minimum number of participants necessary for a study to detect a statistically significant effect of a given size with a specified degree of confidence. Power analysis helps researchers plan appropriate sample sizes before studies begin.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

**Belmont Report Ethics Framework**: The three principles of respect for persons, beneficence, and justice provide the ethical foundation for all research with human subjects. Respect for persons ensures autonomous decision-making through informed consent. Beneficence requires balancing risks and benefits. Justice demands equitable distribution of research burdens and benefits. Together, these principles guide study design, participant recruitment, data collection, and reporting.

**Participant Representativeness Framework**: The validity of research claims depends on how well study participants represent the target user population. This framework considers multiple dimensions: demographic characteristics (age, gender, education), professional background (domain expertise, job role), technical proficiency (computer expertise), and motivational factors (interest, willingness). Mismatches between participant characteristics and target populations limit the generalizability of findings.

**Balancing Control and Ecological Validity**: HCI research faces a fundamental tension between controlled experiments (which produce clear causal inferences but may lack ecological validity) and naturalistic studies (which capture real-world behavior but offer less control). The appropriate balance depends on the research question, the maturity of the research domain, and the practical constraints of the study.

**Demand Characteristics and Researcher Influence Model**: This model recognizes that participants may alter their behavior in response to perceived expectations, researcher presence, experimental context, or compensation structures. Researchers must design studies to minimize these influences through blinding, neutral instructions, and careful management of the researcher-participant relationship.

**Informed Consent as Process Model**: Beyond a one-time document signing, informed consent is an ongoing process that begins with recruitment and continues throughout the study. Participants should have opportunities to ask questions, receive updated information about risks, and withdraw at any point. The consent process should be adapted to the participant population (e.g., simplified language for children, alternative formats for people with disabilities).

**Corporate vs. Academic Research Ethics**: The Facebook emotional contagion study highlighted tensions between corporate and academic research ethics. Private companies may not be subject to the Common Rule, yet their research can affect millions of users. This framework examines the obligations of corporate researchers, the role of IRBs in non-academic settings, and the ethical implications of treating users as research subjects without their knowledge.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Identify Appropriate Participants

Define who your participants should be based on the target user population. Consider demographic, educational, vocational, and avocational characteristics. Determine whether you need experts, novices, or a mix. Consider both domain expertise and computer expertise as separate dimensions. Recognize that tools designed for specific populations (children, elderly, professionals) require participants from those populations.

### Step 2: Determine Sample Size

Use study design, statistical requirements, and available resources to determine the number of participants. For controlled experiments, use power analysis to determine the minimum number needed for statistical significance. Usability studies may succeed with 5-10 participants; ethnographies with 2-3; controlled experiments typically need 15-20+. Always recruit additional participants beyond the minimum for pilot tests, dropouts, and replacements. Consider that studies involving people with disabilities or specialized domain experts may face smaller available pools.

### Step 3: Recruit Participants

Choose recruitment methods appropriate to your participant population:
- **General populations**: Flyers, advertisements on bulletin boards, community websites, social media
- **Specialized populations**: Professional organizations, community groups, email lists, personal contacts
- **Domain experts**: Collaborative arrangements with companies, schools, or organizations
- **Hard-to-reach populations**: Partner with advocacy organizations, offer to visit participants' locations

Screen potential participants to verify they meet study criteria. Consider building a database of willing participants for future studies.

### Step 4: Design Incentive Strategy

Offer fair compensation commensurate with time and participant type:
- Cash payments for students and general participants
- Higher fees for busy professionals and domain experts
- Gifts or toys for children (cash for accompanying parents)
- Consultancy arrangements for expert participants
- Raffles when funds are insufficient for all participants
- Access to developed software as motivation for domain experts

Ensure incentives don't create coercive pressure. Stress that compensation is provided regardless of performance or responses.

### Step 5: Prepare IRB Application

Submit materials to your IRB before beginning research:
- Description of proposed research
- Draft informed consent forms
- Instructions for participants
- Questionnaires and task materials
- Recruitment advertisements
- Data storage and security plans

Start the IRB process early—delays can be significant if materials are incomplete or if the board has scheduled meeting dates. Consider that some IRBs may be unfamiliar with HCI research methods and may need education about ethnography, online studies, and other techniques.

### Step 6: Obtain Informed Consent

Create comprehensive consent documents including all required sections (institution ID, contact info, purpose, procedures, duration, risks, benefits, alternatives, confidentiality, costs, rights, signature). Write at an accessible reading level. Pilot test consent forms for clarity. Ensure consent is truly voluntary—no coercion through compensation structures, power relationships, or implied consequences for non-participation. Provide copies to participants; retain signed originals.

### Step 7: Conduct the Study Respectfully

During the study:
- Treat participants with dignity and respect
- Provide comfortable surroundings and adequate breaks
- Be flexible in scheduling and location
- Never give feedback on participant performance during the study
- Be aware that your presence may influence behavior
- Monitor for signs of discomfort, fatigue, or distress
- Allow participants to withdraw at any time

### Step 8: Protect Privacy Throughout

During and after the study:
- Limit data collection to what is necessary
- Use participant numbers rather than names in records
- Photograph/video interfaces, not faces
- Store data securely (encrypted, locked)
- Remove identifying information from reports
- Be cautious with quotes and descriptions that could identify individuals
- Consider image manipulation (blurring, black bars) for published photos

### Step 9: Handle Special Cases

For **deceptive studies**: justify the necessity of deception, minimize its extent, obtain IRB approval, debrief participants afterward, and allow withdrawal of data. For **longitudinal studies**: plan for attrition, maintain ongoing contact, update consent as needed. For **online studies**: adapt consent processes to digital contexts, address platform-specific privacy concerns, ensure fair compensation for crowdsourced workers.

### Step 10: Report Results Transparently

Describe participant selection methods, sample characteristics, and any limitations in representativeness. Acknowledge potential confounds and biases. Avoid overstating conclusions. Discuss implications for generalizability. Never include identifying information about participants. Share findings with participants when appropriate.

## 4. Decision Criteria

**Choosing Participant Characteristics**: Match participants to the target user population. Consider: age, gender, education, job responsibility, domain expertise, technical experience, and motivation level. The more specific the target population, the more carefully participants must be screened.

**Determining Sample Size**: Use these guidelines:
- Ethnographies and case studies: 2-3 participants (in-depth)
- Usability studies: 5-10+ participants per user group
- Controlled experiments: at least 15-20 participants; use power analysis for precise determination
- Large-scale studies: hundreds to thousands when resources allow
- Add extra participants for pilot tests, dropouts, and replacements

**When Deception is Justified**: Deception may be appropriate when: full disclosure would compromise study realism, the research question cannot be addressed otherwise, risks to participants are minimal, IRB approval is obtained, and debriefing is planned. Always consider alternatives to deception first.

**IRB Review Level**: Determine whether your study requires:
- **Full review**: Studies with more than minimal risk, vulnerable populations, deception
- **Expedited review**: Studies with minimal risk in specified categories
- **Exemption**: Educational tests, surveys, observations of public behavior, existing data studies
Consult your IRB's specific policies and forms.

**Compensation Strategy**: Consider participant type, time commitment, available budget, and potential for coercion. Higher compensation is appropriate for: domain experts, longer sessions, specialized populations, and participants facing transportation challenges. Avoid compensation structures that could be perceived as coercive.

**Privacy Protection Level**: Scale privacy measures to the sensitivity of collected data. Medical, financial, and behavioral data require stronger protections. Consider: data minimization, encryption, secure storage, anonymization in reports, and limited retention. For video/audio recordings, consider: filming interfaces rather than faces, using image manipulation, and providing participants with opt-out options.

**When to Use Online vs. In-Person Methods**: Online methods are appropriate for: large-scale studies, geographically distributed participants, studies of online behavior, and summative evaluation. In-person methods are preferred for: formative evaluation, studies requiring rich qualitative data, sensitive populations, and when environmental control is important.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

**Architectural Modeling Tool Example**: Illustrates the challenge of finding appropriate participants. The study needed architects (not just any computer users), raising questions about whether architecture students would suffice or whether professional architects were required. This demonstrates how study goals drive participant selection criteria.

**Milgram's Obedience Experiment**: Subjects were told they were studying punishment's effect on learning but the true goal was studying obedience. Participants believed they were administering increasingly painful electric shocks to a learner (actually a confederate). Of 40 participants, all continued past the point where the learner stopped responding, and 26 administered maximum shock. The study caused significant distress including nervous laughter, embarrassment, and seizures. This experiment would not be approved by modern IRBs.

**Stanford Prison Experiment**: Zimbardo divided Stanford undergraduates into "guards" and "prisoners" in a mock prison. Both groups completely assumed their roles—guards became humiliating and abusive, prisoners became passive and depressed. One prisoner suffered crying and rage. Originally planned for 2 weeks, the study was terminated after 6 days. Despite being approved by Stanford's review board and consistent with 1973 APA guidelines, it is now considered a cautionary tale about research ethics.

**Facebook Emotional Contagion Study (2014)**: Cornell and Facebook researchers manipulated emotional content in approximately 700,000 users' news feeds without explicit informed consent. Facebook argued that creating an account constituted implicit agreement to research. Cornell's IRB determined the study was not under their jurisdiction as it was conducted by Facebook. The study found that reducing positive posts led to less positive user postings, with comparable effects for negative content. Publication sparked widespread controversy about corporate research ethics, informed consent in social media, and A/B testing ethics.

**Menu Task Performance Studies with Blind Users**: Researchers collaborated with the National Federation of the Blind (NFB) to recruit experienced screen-reader users. NFB was paid as a consultant and provided office space for the study. Participants received higher-than-customary compensation due to the specialized population. This study found that blind users, like sighted users, performed better with broad, shallow menu structures.

**Nuremberg Code and Tuskegee Study**: Historical examples that shaped modern research ethics. Nazi medical experiments led to the Nuremberg Code establishing informed consent principles. The Tuskegee Syphilis Study, where black men with syphilis were deceived and denied treatment for 40 years, damaged public trust in research—particularly among Black Americans. These cases demonstrate why ethical protections are essential.

**Virtual Reprise of Milgram's Experiment**: Subjects administered virtual shocks to a female virtual human in an immersive environment. Even though participants knew they were interacting with a computer-generated avatar, they responded as if working with a real person, particularly when they could see the avatar. This demonstrates how virtual environments can study obedience phenomena without the ethical concerns of the original experiment.

**Security and Usability Deception Study**: Participants were told the study aimed to "help make online banking better" when it actually studied security behavior. Some participants used their own bank accounts; others role-played. The study found that people using their own accounts behaved more securely than role-players. This illustrates how deception can improve ecological validity but also raises questions about demand characteristics.

**Indiana University Phishing Study**: Researchers harvested email addresses from publicly available sources and conducted a phishing attack on students. Arguing that disclosure would invalidate results, they worked closely with their IRB to design a protocol that proceeded without explicit consent, requiring extensive review of regulations and legislation.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

**Using Non-Representative Participants**: Testing with easily found participants (e.g., undergraduate students) when the target population has different characteristics produces results that may not generalize. Even if specific findings are interesting, claims about the target population's behavior are limited. Always match participants to the target user population.

**Insufficient Sample Size**: Studies with too few participants may miss important effects. Very small studies (n < 5) cannot produce statistically significant results for controlled experiments. Use power analysis to determine minimum sample sizes. Add extra participants for pilot tests and dropouts.

**Coerced Participation**: Compensation structures, power relationships (e.g., instructor-student), or implied consequences for non-participation can create coercion. Always ensure participation is truly voluntary. Provide alternative ways to earn course credit if offering credit for participation. Stress that compensation is provided regardless of responses.

**Inadequate Informed Consent**: Consent forms that are too long, too technical, or too vague fail to truly inform participants. Write at an accessible reading level. Pilot test consent forms. Cover all required sections. Ensure participants understand they can withdraw at any time.

**Ignoring Demand Characteristics**: Participants may try to please researchers by providing expected responses. Minimize demand characteristics by: not revealing study hypotheses, using neutral instructions, blinding participants to researcher preferences, and not providing performance feedback during the study.

**Not Obtaining IRB Approval**: Conducting research with human subjects without IRB approval violates institutional and federal regulations. Start the IRB process early to avoid delays. Even studies that may qualify for exemption must be reviewed by IRB staff.

**Inadequate Privacy Protection**: Failing to protect participant data can harm individuals and damage trust in research. Implement appropriate security measures for data storage, anonymize data in reports, and be cautious with quotes and descriptions that could identify individuals in small populations.

**Overstating Conclusions**: Drawing stronger conclusions than the data supports, particularly regarding generalizability. If participants are not representative of the target population, acknowledge this limitation. If the study has methodological limitations, discuss them honestly.

**Ignoring Confounding Variables**: In multi-group studies, failing to ensure groups are comparable in all relevant attributes except the one being studied. If groups differ in multiple ways, it becomes impossible to determine which factor caused observed differences.

**Neglecting Participant Comfort**: Uncomfortable surroundings, inadequate breaks, inflexible scheduling, and intimidating researcher behavior can affect both participant well-being and data quality. Treat participants as valued partners in the research process.

**Not Debriefing After Deceptive Studies**: Failing to debrief participants about the true nature of deceptive studies leaves participants unaware of how they were misled. Debriefing allows participants to understand the research, ask questions, and withdraw their data if they wish.

**Ignoring Corporate Research Ethics**: Companies conducting A/B tests or manipulating user experiences may not be subject to the Common Rule but still have ethical obligations to users. The Facebook study demonstrates how corporate research without informed consent can damage public trust and raise serious ethical concerns.

**Treating IRB as Adversary**: Viewing the IRB as an obstacle rather than an ally can lead to adversarial relationships and poor outcomes. IRBs protect researchers and institutions from problems associated with research that goes wrong. Approach IRB interactions from a collegial perspective and seek their guidance when questions arise.

**Not Building Participant Databases**: Failing to maintain records of willing participants for future studies leads to repeated recruitment efforts. Screening and enrolling participants is time-consuming; a database of pre-screened, willing participants can significantly reduce the effort for subsequent studies.
