# Chapter 9: Field Studies and Ethnography — Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

**Ethnography** is a research methodology rooted in anthropology that involves in-depth observation, participation, and immersion in a human group, culture, or context. The goal is to develop a rich description of activities, interactions, beliefs, roles, and goals within that group. In HCI, ethnography is used to understand the context of technology usage in naturalistic settings such as homes, workplaces, schools, and online communities.

**Field Study** refers to any research conducted in a natural setting rather than a laboratory. Field studies in HCI observe how people use technology in their actual environments, capturing contextual factors that controlled experiments cannot reveal. Field studies may or may not involve the deep immersion characteristic of ethnography.

**Participation** is a critical practice in ethnography where researchers engage directly with the group being studied rather than remaining detached observers. Participation removes the need for intermediaries and allows researchers to experience phenomena firsthand, providing data richness impossible to obtain through other methods.

**Triangulation** is the process of using corroborating evidence from multiple data sources, methods, or perspectives to increase confidence in the validity of research conclusions. In ethnography, triangulation combines interviews, observations, and document analysis to build a comprehensive understanding.

**Situated Action** is a framework describing all action as a product of the context in which it is taken. Lucy Suchman's famous study used this framework to analyze how differences between human mental models and expert system models led to communication breakdowns in photocopier use.

**Informants** are group members who serve as key sources of information for ethnographic researchers. They help researchers orient to the environment, introduce them to others, and provide perspectives on group dynamics. Researchers must critically evaluate informant information since informants provide viewpoints rather than objective truths.

**Inductive Research** is research that moves from raw data to pattern identification and then to general theories explaining those patterns. Ethnography is fundamentally inductive, contrasting with hypothesis-driven deductive research that tests well-defined predictions.

**Complete Participant** refers to a research role where the ethnographer becomes a full member of the group being studied, learning "from the inside." This approach maximizes immersion but risks the researcher "going native" and losing objectivity.

**Complete Observer** is the opposite extreme where the researcher observes without direct interaction, remaining detached from subjects. While this avoids loss of objectivity, it sacrifices the rich understanding that participation provides.

**Virtual Ethnography** applies ethnographic methods to online communities and virtual environments. The virtual nature of these communities offers unique opportunities for participation and identity manipulation while presenting challenges around verifying identities and maintaining ethical standards.

**Participatory Design** is a design philosophy that involves users in every stage of design, from understanding problems to evaluating prototypes. While sharing ethnography's interest in direct engagement, participatory design focuses on building solutions, whereas ethnography focuses on understanding problems.

**Stranger-Handlers** are individuals who make it their business to work with newcomers to a group. They introduce researchers to others and show them around, but may provide a biased or slanted perspective emphasizing certain details while omitting others.

**Deviants** are outcasts within a group who may try to use the researcher's presence to gain attention, validate their under-appreciated role, or denigrate their enemies. Researchers should be wary of both stranger-handlers and deviants as initial contacts.

**Probes** are items presented to participants during ethnographic interviews designed to provoke reaction and spark conversation, helping researchers elicit deeper responses about the context being studied.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

**Situated Action Framework** posits that human action is fundamentally shaped by the context in which it occurs. Lucy Suchman applied this framework to HCI by showing that users' interactions with a photocopier expert system failed because the system's model did not account for the situated nature of human problem-solving. This framework argues that plans are resources for action rather than determinants of action.

**Inductive vs. Deductive Research Paradigm** represents a fundamental methodological divide. Ethnography operates primarily inductively, building theories from observed data patterns. This contrasts with experimental HCI research that tests specific hypotheses. The inductive approach allows discovery of unexpected phenomena but makes generalization more difficult. Some ethnographers reject theory-building entirely, arguing that researchers should simply describe what they see, while others contend that researcher participation inevitably introduces interpretive frameworks.

**Contextual Understanding Model** emphasizes that successful design of complex or novel interfaces requires understanding the full context surrounding where information systems will be used and who will use them. This includes organizational structures, work practices, communication patterns, policies, and cultural factors. Ethnography provides this contextual understanding that simpler methods like surveys and interviews cannot adequately capture.

**Complete Participation Continuum** frames ethnographic roles as a spectrum from complete observer to complete participant, with various intermediate positions. Researchers may adopt multiple roles throughout a single project, often beginning as observers and moving toward greater participation as understanding develops. The choice of role involves tradeoffs between depth of understanding and risk of bias.

**Triangulation Theory** holds that confidence in research conclusions increases when multiple data sources converge on the same findings. In ethnography, this means combining interviews, observations, documents, and artifacts. Multiple informants and multiple observers further strengthen triangulation by reducing individual bias. The theoretical assumption is that no single data source is sufficient for understanding complex human phenomena.

**Participatory Design Theory** extends ethnographic engagement into the design process. It is based on three key premises: (1) user tasks may not be well understood; (2) the users themselves may not be well understood; (3) even minor task completion errors can have catastrophic consequences. Participatory design uses ethnographic methods to understand problems and then intensely involves participants in building solutions, making it particularly valuable for high-risk, high-payoff design projects.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Select a Site or Group of Interest

Identify groups that are interesting, logistically feasible, and committed to supporting the research goals. Consider whether you want representative sites or extreme cases. Recognize potential barriers to access: healthcare systems may have data protection requirements, schools may restrict access, and government or military installations may require security clearances. You may need multiple stages of approval including IRB approval, security checks, confidentiality agreements, and similar formalities.

### Step 2: Choose a Participation Role

Determine the appropriate level of involvement on the participation continuum:

- **Complete Participant**: Full immersion in the group, potentially concealing researcher identity. Most informative but most ethically complex and time-intensive.
- **Participant-Observer**: Combines participation with observation, disclosing researcher role while getting varying levels of group involvement. Most common approach in HCI.
- **Complete Observer**: Observes without direct interaction. Least intrusive but provides shallowest understanding.
- **Shadowing**: Following group members as they go about their activities, asking questions as needed for clarification.

Most HCI ethnographic projects adopt an intermediate approach, often beginning as observers and increasing participation as understanding develops.

### Step 3: Build Relationships and Make Initial Contact

Begin with a small number of carefully chosen contacts who can orient you to the environment and introduce you to others. Be wary of stranger-handlers who may provide biased perspectives and deviants who may seek to exploit your presence. Choose informants who are trustworthy, well-respected, and capable of providing good information. Avoid becoming too closely associated with any single faction. Present yourself as nonthreatening and genuinely curious.

### Step 4: Conduct Iterative Data Collection

Employ multiple data collection techniques in an ongoing, fluid manner:

- **Informal interviews**: Open-ended conversations that may not feel like formal interviews. Use probes to provoke reaction. Ask questions that invite comparisons, contrasts, and detailed explanations.
- **Observation**: Record relevant details including time, place, identities, context, behaviors, and interactions. Distinguish between observation and interpretation. Challenge yourself to broaden the scope of what you notice.
- **Documents and artifacts**: Examine records, archives, pictures, letters, emails, deliverable documents, and tools. These provide relatively static data sources but may be incomplete or biased.
- **Field notes**: Record observations systematically, noting familiar events in terms of identified patterns and unfamiliar events worthy of consideration. Write notes as soon as possible after observations.

### Step 5: Analyze Data Iteratively

Analysis combines qualitative and quantitative techniques. As you examine data to identify patterns, new questions will arise. This analysis is a precursor to further data collection: uncertain interpretations lead to follow-up questions, and new observations open new lines of investigation. This iterative cycle of collection-analysis-collection continues until resources are exhausted or sufficient understanding is achieved.

### Step 6: Build Models and Theories

Develop robust explanations based on all collected data. Avoid cherry-picking data that supports your preferred model while ignoring contradictory evidence. Seek out data that might disconfirm your models. Compare alternative models to understand strengths and weaknesses. Use multiple informants and multiple observers to increase validity.

### Step 7: Report Results

Describe goals, methods, justification for group selection, data collection and analysis methods, raw data, and analytical results. Use matrices, charts, and figures for quantitative data. Discuss rival explanations. Tell a story with direct quotes where appropriate. Consider sharing draft reports with participants for reality checks. Address privacy and confidentiality by anonymizing location details and suppressing identifying information.

## 4. Decision Criteria

**When to Use Ethnography**: Ethnography is most appropriate when:
- You need to understand a complex, unfamiliar context or domain
- Users' tasks, work practices, or environments are not well understood
- Simple methods like surveys and interviews are insufficient because you don't know enough to ask the right questions
- The human, social, and organizational aspects of a system are critical to its success
- You need to understand system requirements for a novel or complex interface
- You want to understand how technology is actually used in natural settings

**Ethnography vs. Case Study**: Choose ethnography when you need deeper, more immersive engagement over a longer period. Case studies are often hypothesis-driven and use theory to guide questions, while ethnographies are more inductive and exploratory. Ethnographies involve more fluid and extensive use of observation and participation than case studies.

**Ethnography vs. Usability Testing**: Usability testing is a short-term evaluation method focused on finding and fixing interface flaws, generally involving a few individuals observed in controlled conditions. Ethnography is a longer-term approach focused on understanding problems, contexts, cultures, and group interactions. Usability testing evaluates potential solutions; ethnography understands the problem.

**Ethnography vs. Participatory Design**: Both involve direct engagement with users, but ethnography focuses on understanding people, groups, processes, and beliefs, while participatory design uses ethnographic understanding to build technology solutions. Participatory design is more resource-intensive and is typically used for high-risk projects with complex or poorly understood user needs.

**Choosing a Participation Role**: Consider:
- Access constraints (medical, legal, security requirements)
- Time and resource availability
- Risk of "going native" or losing objectivity
- Ethical considerations around concealment
- Practical limitations on what participation is possible
- The nature of the research questions

**Site Selection Criteria**:
- Representativeness vs. extreme cases for the research goals
- Logistical feasibility (travel, access, time commitment)
- Group willingness and commitment
- Potential barriers (security, confidentiality, access restrictions)
- Balance between familiarity (easier access) and fresh perspective (less bias)

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

**Hospital Information System (Introductory Example)**: The chapter opens with a scenario of designing user interfaces for an intensive-care unit information system in a foreign country. This illustrates why ethnography is necessary: surveys and interviews are insufficient when you don't know enough to ask the right questions. The example reveals how ethnographic observation uncovers important contextual details, such as hospitals referring to patients by bed number rather than ID—a design consideration that would be invisible without in-context observation.

**Lucy Suchman's Photocopier Study**: Perhaps the most famous example of ethnography in HCI. Suchman observed users attempting to complete photocopying tasks with an expert system designed to help them. Through video analysis using the situated action framework, she demonstrated how differences between human mental models and the expert system's model led to communication breakdowns and task failures. This study remains influential both for its discussion of human-machine communication and as an example of ethnography's utility in HCI.

**Corporate Multitasking Study**: Researchers shadowed 19 workers at a large US corporation for 550 hours, collecting over 13,000 events to understand how workers switch between tasks, communication partners, and media. The finding that coordinating with multiple people was stressful and difficult led to suggestions for designing communication systems that identify high-coordination interruptions. This detailed behavioral record would have been impossible to collect through other methods.

**Insurance Claims Adjuster Study**: Ethnographic methods were used to understand how insurance claims adjusters do their work, with special focus on fraudulent claims. Researchers discovered process barriers like poor documentation and identified the heuristics adjusters use to detect fraud. This understanding was then used to develop software tools for insurance fraud detection.

**Home Technology Studies**: Cross-cultural ethnographic studies revealed that country, culture, and religion profoundly impact how technology is used in homes. Whirlpool washing machines failed in southern India because traditional thin-fabric clothes were shredded by machines designed for Western clothing. Gender issues were also observed, with women performing a majority of domestic tasks even when working equally outside the home.

**GPS Navigation Study**: Researchers went on rides with GPS users to understand how in-car navigation systems changed perceptions of driving and the environment. Analysis showed GPS made users both less engaged (less attention to turns and landmarks) and more engaged (awareness of nearby parks and attractions not visible from the road).

**iPhone User Study**: An ethnographic study tracked six iPhone purchasers through anticipation, orientation, incorporation, and identification phases, building a model of how users move from initial excitement to integrating the device into their identity.

**Virtual Ethnography - Star Wars Galaxies**: Two researchers created characters in the multiplayer online game and logged in for minimum 4 hours per week over 3 months. As complete participants, they could study genuine interactions in virtual social spaces without revealing their researcher identity.

**Online Dating Research**: Multiple studies examined online dating communities. One found that 48% of participants had inaccurate height and 59% had inaccurate weight in their profiles. Another analyzed 236,000 messages from 29,000 users, finding 78% of initial contacts received no response. These studies illustrate both the richness of ethnographic approaches and the ethical complexities of researching online communities.

**Bob's ACL Bulletin Board**: Jenny Preece, an HCI researcher who tore her ACL, joined an online support community and later conducted ethnographically informed research on empathic communication. Her student continued the study as a strict observer rather than participant, illustrating how different participation roles affect the research approach.

**Firefighter Navigation Study**: Researchers donned firefighting gear and participated in simulations to understand how firefighters use improvisation and collaboration to navigate in smoke-filled environments. This study of ubiquitous computing requirements required going into unexpected and physically demanding settings.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

**Confusing Ethnography with Simple Observation**: Ethnography involves much more than just watching—it requires participation, relationship building, iterative data collection and analysis, and deep contextual immersion. Simply observing without engaging misses the core value of ethnographic methods.

**Going Native**: Researchers who become too immersed in the group may lose their ability to maintain critical distance and objectivity. When the researcher's identity as a group member overwhelms their training as a researcher, they may fail to notice important phenomena or may adopt the group's biases. Strategies to mitigate this include bias-awareness training, rigorous documentation, and using multiple researchers.

**Covert Research Ethics Violations**: Concealing researcher identity in private settings may mean deceiving group members about the reasons for participation, which is generally considered unethical. While covert participation may be acceptable in public settings, misrepresenting one's goals to access groups one could not otherwise join raises serious ethical concerns.

**Over-reliance on Single Informants**: Relying too heavily on one or a few informants can produce a distorted view of the group. Stranger-handlers may present biased perspectives, deviants may exploit the researcher's presence, and even well-meaning informants provide viewpoints rather than objective truths. Use multiple informants with diverse backgrounds, perspectives, and demographics.

**Researcher Bias**: Individuals filter observations through their own history, experience, and expertise. Researchers may pay more attention to certain details while ignoring others in ways they are unaware of. Mitigation strategies include bias-awareness training, careful methodology documentation, multiple observers, and regularly challenging oneself to broaden observational scope.

**Cherry-Picking Data**: Researchers may unconsciously select data that supports their preferred model while ignoring contradictory evidence. A robust ethnographic analysis must actively seek out disconfirming evidence and consider alternative models. If observations are inconsistent with your model, consider revising the model rather than dismissing the data.

**Neglecting Relationship Building**: Failing to invest time in building trust and relationships with group members can severely limit data access and quality. Researchers must understand group conventions and norms, be respectful of participants' needs, ensure benefits outweigh costs for participants, and avoid making people feel threatened.

**Inadequate Field Notes**: Taking notes that are either too detailed (overwhelming the researcher and preventing observation) or too sparse (missing important details). The challenge intensifies because you never know when something interesting will happen. Write notes as soon as possible after observations and seek validating evidence for any observations recorded long after the fact.

**Over-generalization**: Ethnography focuses on understanding specific contexts without generalization as a goal. Treating ethnographic findings as universally applicable misunderstands the methodology's purpose. The value lies in deep contextual understanding, not broad generalizability.

**Confusing Ethnography with Participatory Design**: While both involve direct user engagement, ethnography focuses on understanding problems, contexts, and group dynamics, while participatory design uses that understanding to build solutions. An ethnographic study that primarily aims to develop technology may actually be participatory design using ethnographic methods—understanding this distinction affects research goals, reporting, and evaluation.

**Ignoring Practical Impact on Participants**: Researchers who are so focused on their data needs that they forget the practical impact of their presence on the group may damage relationships and lose access. Make sure the research benefits outweigh the costs for participants, and consider compensating participants when benefits are not immediately apparent.

**Failing to Navigate Access Barriers**: Underestimating the time and effort needed to gain access to research sites. Healthcare, educational, government, and military settings may require IRB approval, security checks, confidentiality agreements, and other formalities. Planning for these requirements early prevents delays.
