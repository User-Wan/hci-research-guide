# Chapter 5: Usability Testing - Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

### Survey Research
Survey research is a method that allows researchers to collect data from a large number of participants efficiently. Surveys are frequently used in HCI research to describe populations, explain behaviors, get feedback about user experiences, measure attitudes, awareness, and intent, measure characteristics of users, and explore uncharted waters. When structured properly, well-tested, and implemented appropriately, surveys yield data with high validity.

### Target Population
The target population refers to the specific group of individuals from whom survey responses are needed. It is rare that "anyone can respond to the survey." Survey responses usually need to come from a specific population being studied—for instance, people of a certain age, users of a certain software application, people who work in a certain industry, or people who have a certain disability.

### Sampling Frame
The sampling frame is the actual list or set of listings used to define potential survey respondents. It might be a phone book, email list, membership directory, or other source of contact information. The sampling frame should ideally include all members of the target population, though this is often not perfectly achievable.

### Probabilistic Sampling
Probabilistic sampling (random sampling) is a structured method where it is known exactly how likely it is for a participant to be selected for the sample. Each member of the population has an equal, greater-than-zero chance of being selected, and everyone selected receives the same invitation to participate. This method allows researchers to make statistically accurate estimates for a population.

### Nonprobabilistic Sampling
Nonprobabilistic sampling includes approaches where the probability of selection is not known or not equal. This includes volunteer opt-in panels, self-selected surveys, convenience sampling, and snowball recruiting. While not as statistically rigorous as probabilistic sampling, nonprobabilistic samples are widely used and considered valid in HCI research.

### Stratified Sampling
Stratified sampling divides the entire population into separate subpopulations (strata) and selects a separate sample from within each subpopulation. This ensures appropriate representation from each subset of the user population and can help when subpopulations have unequal representation.

### Response Rate
Response rate is the percentage of people who respond to a survey out of the total number invited to participate. Low response rates can introduce bias if non-respondents differ systematically from respondents. Techniques to increase response rates include multiple contacts, incentives, and establishing credibility.

### Oversampling
Oversampling occurs when the number of survey responses is large in proportion to the estimated or perceived population size. While not all researchers agree that oversampling increases validity, having a large response can reduce the likelihood of excluding any segment of the population.

### Open-Ended Questions
Open-ended questions give respondents complete flexibility in their answers, allowing them to express views in their own words. They are useful for understanding phenomena in depth but require more complex data analysis and careful wording to elicit useful responses.

### Closed-Ended Questions
Closed-ended questions provide predefined response options for respondents to choose from. They can have ordered response categories (like Likert scales) or unordered categories (like multiple choice). They are easier to analyze statistically but may not capture unexpected responses.

### Double-Barreled Questions
Double-barreled questions ask two separate, and possibly related, questions in one item. For example: "How long have you used the Word processing software and which advanced features have you used?" These questions need to be separated to avoid confusion and invalid responses.

### Contingent Questions
Contingent questions are questions where the respondent's need to respond is contingent on their response to a previous question. For example, if a respondent answers "yes" to using a software application, they may be directed to answer additional questions about that application.

### Pilot Testing
Pilot testing (pretesting) involves administering a survey to a small group before the main study to identify problems with question wording, survey layout, or instructions. It helps ensure questions are clear, unambiguous, and unbiased, and that the survey measures what it claims to measure.

### Reliability
Reliability is the determination of whether a survey measures constructs consistently across time. Methods for measuring internal reliability include having the same question asked multiple times in different ways, with Cronbach's alpha coefficient commonly used to assess consistency.

### Exploratory Factor Analysis
Exploratory factor analysis is a statistical technique used to evaluate survey questions after data collection. It creates artificial dimensions that correlate highly with sets of survey question data, helping researchers determine which items are most predictive and relevant based on factor loadings.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

### Total Survey Error Framework
The total survey error framework identifies various sources of error in survey research: sampling error (insufficient responses), coverage error (not all population members have equal chance of selection), measurement error (poorly worded questions), and nonresponse error (differences between respondents and non-respondents). Understanding these errors helps improve survey validity.

### Cognitive Interviewing Framework
Cognitive interviewing focuses on understanding how respondents interpret and answer survey questions. It involves think-aloud protocols where respondents verbalize their thought processes. This framework helps identify problems with question wording, comprehension, and response strategies.

### Sampling Theory
Sampling theory provides the statistical foundation for selecting representative samples from populations. It includes probability theory, sampling distributions, and confidence intervals. For simple random samples, 384 responses may yield 95% confidence with ±5% margin of error, regardless of population size.

### Response Process Model
The response process model describes the cognitive steps respondents go through: comprehension (understanding the question), retrieval (recalling relevant information), judgment (forming an estimate), and response (selecting an answer). This model helps identify potential sources of error at each stage.

### Mode Effects Framework
Mode effects framework examines how different survey modes (paper, online, telephone, face-to-face) affect response patterns. Different modes may introduce different biases due to factors like interviewer presence, visual presentation, and respondent comfort. Understanding mode effects helps researchers select the most appropriate mode.

### Psychometric Theory
Psychometric theory provides the foundation for developing reliable and valid measurement instruments. It includes classical test theory and item response theory, guiding the development and evaluation of survey scales through concepts like reliability and validity.

### Nonprobability Sampling Validity Framework
This framework acknowledges that while nonprobability samples cannot make population estimates, they can still provide valid data through techniques like collecting demographic data, oversampling, and using multiple research methods. Validity is established through representativeness checks and large response sizes relative to the population.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Define Research Objectives
Clearly articulate what you want to learn from the survey. Identify specific research questions or hypotheses. Ensure that survey research is the appropriate method for answering these questions, considering alternatives like experiments, interviews, or observations.

### Step 2: Identify Target Population
Define the population from whom you need responses. Consider demographic characteristics, geographic location, profession, disability status, or technology usage. Develop clear inclusion criteria specifying who qualifies to participate.

### Step 3: Determine Sampling Method
Choose between probabilistic and nonprobabilistic sampling based on your goals and practical constraints. If making population estimates is critical and a sampling frame exists, use random sampling. If exploring new phenomena or studying hard-to-reach populations, nonprobabilistic methods may be appropriate.

### Step 4: Locate Sampling Frame
Identify how you will contact potential respondents. For defined populations, look for membership directories, professional organization lists, software registration lists, or social networking groups. For broader populations, consider intercept sampling, self-selected surveys, or snowball recruiting.

### Step 5: Develop Survey Questions
Create clear, unambiguous questions that address your research objectives. Use appropriate question types (open-ended, closed-ended with ordered/unordered responses). Avoid double-barreled questions, negative words, biased wording, and hot-button words. Consider using validated existing instruments when available.

### Step 6: Structure the Survey
Organize the survey with clear instructions, appropriate navigation, and logical question order. Group related questions together, place interesting questions first, and save demographic questions for last. Use contingent questions appropriately with clear directions. Ensure adequate white space and readable formatting.

### Step 7: Pilot Test the Survey
Conduct a three-stage pilot test: (1) review by knowledgeable colleagues, (2) interviews with potential respondents about clarity and motivation, (3) pilot study where potential respondents complete the entire survey. Revise based on feedback, paying attention to unanswered questions, multiple answers, and "other" responses.

### Step 8: Determine Sample Size
For probabilistic sampling, calculate required sample size based on desired confidence level and margin of error (e.g., 384 for 95% confidence, ±5% margin). For nonprobabilistic sampling, aim for large response sizes relative to the population, with 30 responses as a baseline minimum.

### Step 9: Distribute Survey
Send introductory letters establishing credibility and importance. Use multiple contacts: precontact letter, actual survey mailing, thank you/reminder, replacement survey to non-respondents, and final contact using different mode. Provide self-addressed return envelopes for paper surveys.

### Step 10: Collect and Clean Data
Collect responses and check for incomplete, invalid, or duplicate submissions. Clean the data by removing responses that don't meet inclusion criteria or have excessive missing data. Organize data for analysis, separating quantitative and qualitative data.

### Step 11: Analyze Data
Analyze quantitative data using descriptive statistics (percentages, ratios, matrices) and inferential statistics as appropriate. For qualitative data from open-ended questions, conduct content analysis to identify themes and patterns. Use exploratory factor analysis to evaluate survey items if appropriate.

### Step 12: Interpret and Report Findings
Interpret results considering sampling method, response rate, and potential biases. Acknowledge limitations and discuss implications for theory and practice. Report findings with appropriate visualizations and statistical information.

## 4. Decision Criteria

### When to Use Surveys
Use surveys when you need to collect data from a large number of people efficiently, when measuring attitudes, awareness, intent, or user characteristics, when making population estimates, or when exploring new phenomena. Surveys are less appropriate for precise measurements or solely identifying usability problems.

### When to Use Probabilistic Sampling
Use probabilistic sampling when you need to make statistically accurate population estimates, when a complete sampling frame exists, and when resources allow for random selection. This is appropriate for census-like studies or when strict representativeness is required.

### When to Use Nonprobabilistic Sampling
Use nonprobabilistic sampling when probabilistic sampling is not feasible or necessary. This is appropriate when exploring new user populations or phenomena, when no centralized list of potential respondents exists, when the population is hard to reach, or when surveys are used in conjunction with other methods.

### When to Use Stratification
Use stratification when you want to ensure adequate representation from specific subgroups within your population. This is appropriate when subpopulations have unequal representation, when you want to study specific subgroups in detail, or when you need to oversample underrepresented groups.

### When to Use Open-Ended Questions
Use open-ended questions when you want to explore topics in depth, capture unanticipated responses, or allow respondents to express views in their own words. They are appropriate for exploratory research, complex topics, and when generating rich qualitative data.

### When to Use Closed-Ended Questions
Use closed-ended questions when you want to collect quantitative data that can be easily analyzed statistically, when response options can be predefined, when comparing responses across participants, and when minimizing respondent burden.

### When to Use Existing Survey Instruments
Use existing validated survey instruments when available for your research topic. This saves development time and leverages previously tested reliability and validity. Existing instruments can often be modified minimally to fit specific research needs.

### When to Use Paper Surveys
Use paper surveys when the target population has limited internet access, when distributing in physical settings (meetings, classrooms), when studying non-use of technology, or when respondents prefer paper format. Paper surveys may be necessary for certain populations like older adults or those with limited digital literacy.

### When to Use Online Surveys
Use online surveys when you need to reach geographically dispersed populations quickly and cost-effectively, when automatic data recording is beneficial, when anonymity is important, or when using web-based survey tools for easy distribution and analysis.

### When to Conduct Pilot Testing
Always conduct pilot testing before administering the main survey. This is essential for identifying confusing questions, testing survey layout, estimating completion time, and ensuring the survey measures what it intends to measure. Even minimal pilot testing with a few colleagues or potential respondents is valuable.

### When to Use Incentives
Use incentives when response rates are a concern, when the survey is lengthy or burdensome, or when the target population is difficult to reach. Incentives can include monetary payments, gift cards, prize drawings, or donations to charity. Consider what motivates your specific population.

### When to Use Snowball Sampling
Use snowball sampling when the population of interest is very small and hard to "break into," when individuals in the population know each other well, or when an outside researcher may lack credibility. Community members suggesting participation may be more effective than researcher recruitment.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

### Case 1: Flickr Tagging Study
Researchers studied tagging behavior on Flickr by combining a survey with data logging. They contacted a random sample of 1373 users who had posted at least five unique tags, received 237 valid responses, and collected automated data on the number of tags used. This illustrates combining survey data with computer-collected data for more accurate measurements.

### Case 2: Flickr Photo Sharing Study
Eight years later, another study recruited active Flickr users through Amazon's Mechanical Turk to respond to a survey about photo sharing. Researchers collected survey data from 96 participants and aggregate data from 638,930 active users. This shows how surveys can be combined with large-scale automated data collection.

### Case 3: IBM Beehive Social Networking Study
A study of IBM's Beehive enterprise social networking system randomly selected 500 users from 38,000 registered users based on having logged in during the last week. Of the 500 selected, 415 logged in during the study period, 258 responded, and 230 submitted valid surveys after data cleaning. This illustrates random sampling from a well-defined population.

### Case 4: US Census Web Form Testing
The US Census Bureau tested a web-based form during the 2000 census, allowing people with the short form to complete it online using an ID number from their paper form. Due to security concerns, they did not offer web forms in 2010 but used web surveys for reinterviewing. This shows how even large-scale government surveys adapt to technology.

### Case 5: American Library Association Internet Survey
The ALA has sponsored annual/biennial surveys on Internet access in US public libraries since 1994. The 2011-12 survey included 16,776 library outlets, an 8790 sample frame stratified by state and metropolitan status, and 7260 responses (82.5% response rate). This illustrates organizational sampling frames and stratification.

### Case 6: Technology and Friendship After Moving Study
Researchers studied how technology influences maintaining friendships after residential moves. A sample of 6000 individuals was chosen from the US Postal Service's Change of Address Database, stratified so 1/3 had local moves and 2/3 had longer distance moves. Of 6000 selected, 1779 (32%) responded to the first survey. This demonstrates stratification to oversample a subgroup of interest.

### Case 7: Web Credibility Study
Fogg et al. collected 1441 survey responses in 1 week through charitable groups and a news media organization to study web credibility. After discarding inadequate responses, 1410 valid surveys were analyzed. The survey collected extensive demographic data (age, gender, country, education, income, Internet experience) to confirm representativeness. This illustrates using demographic data and oversampling for validity.

### Case 8: Screen Reader User Survey
WebAIM's Screen Reader Survey has been conducted since 2008 to learn about screen reader user preferences. The 6th survey in 2015 received 2515 responses. While theoretically random sampling might be possible, the competitive screen reader market makes company collaboration unlikely. This illustrates when self-selected surveys are the only feasible approach.

### Case 9: Georgia Tech WWW User Surveys
From 1994 to 1998, Georgia Tech conducted semiannual surveys of the entire web population. Banner ads were placed on search engines, news sites, advertising networks, mailing lists, and newsgroups inviting everyone to participate. The final survey received 5022 responses. This is an early example of self-selected web surveys.

### Case 10: Down Syndrome Computer Usage Study
When no research existed on how individuals with Down syndrome use computers, researchers developed a 56-question survey covering demographics, usage patterns, interaction techniques, and other electronic devices. Parents were recruited through the National Down Syndrome Congress and National Down Syndrome Society, collecting 561 surveys to establish baseline data. This illustrates surveys for uninvestigated populations.

### Case 11: Student Sustainable Design Perceptions
A survey study collected 435 surveys from a cross-section of majors (not just computer science) to learn about student perceptions of sustainable interaction design. This illustrates appropriate use of student populations when students are the actual target of research.

### Case 12: Multinational Smartphone Locking Study
Harbach et al. studied smartphone locking in eight different countries with over 8000 survey responses. This required collecting detailed demographic and cultural data related to each country and consulting guides on cross-cultural HCI research. This illustrates challenges of multinational survey research.

These validated instruments can be used or modified rather than creating new surveys from scratch.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

### Error 1: Using Surveys When Not Appropriate
Surveys are often used not because they are the most appropriate method but because they are the easiest method. Researchers quickly write surveys without sufficient pilot testing, distribute to convenient populations, and claim generalizability. Surveys are less appropriate for precise measurements or solely identifying usability problems.

### Error 2: Poor Question Wording
Common problems include double-barreled questions (asking two questions in one), negative words causing confusion, biased wording (e.g., "Don't you agree that..."), and hot-button words leading to biased responses. All questions should be carefully examined for these issues.

### Error 3: Insufficient Pilot Testing
Many researchers skip or minimize pilot testing. A three-stage process is ideal: review by knowledgeable colleagues, interviews with potential respondents, and a full pilot study. Even minimal pilot testing is necessary to identify confusing questions and survey design problems.

### Error 4: Not Establishing Credibility
Failing to establish the importance and credibility of the survey reduces response rates. Introductory letters should identify the sponsor, explain why the research is important, state expected timeframes, and ideally include endorsement from trusted authorities within the community.

### Error 5: Inadequate Response Rate Efforts
Single contacts yield low response rates. A multistep process is needed: precontact letter, actual survey, thank you/reminder postcard, replacement survey to non-respondents, and final contact using different mode. Self-addressed return envelopes and incentives can also help.

### Error 6: Confusing Survey with Questionnaire
Some researchers confuse the questionnaire (list of questions) with the survey (complete methodological approach including sampling, reminders, and incentives). The questionnaire is only one element of a well-done survey; attention to all methodological aspects is needed for valid data.

### Error 7: Recall Bias in Usage Questions
Questions about patterns of usage in the distant past rely on respondents accurately recalling events, which can lead to biased data. For factual data that can be collected automatically (like number of tasks completed), computer-collected data may be more accurate than survey self-reports.

### Error 8: Not Collecting Demographic Data
For nonprobabilistic samples, failing to collect demographic data makes it impossible to assess representativeness. Even basic demographic data on age, gender, education, job responsibility, or computer usage helps establish validity and representativeness of self-selected responses.

### Error 9: Insufficient Sample Size for Nonprobability Samples
While there's no magic number, 20 survey responses may not be sufficient for nonprobability samples due to potential biases. One researcher suggests 30 responses as a baseline minimum. The response must be large in the context of the population of interest to reduce likelihood of excluding population segments.

### Error 10: Poor Survey Layout and Navigation
Surveys need clear instructions, appropriate navigation, and logical question order. Paper surveys need adequate white space, readable fonts, and clear formatting. Web surveys need appropriate interface widgets (option buttons for single choice, checkboxes for multiple choice). Contingent questions need clear directions.

### Error 11: Placing Demographic Questions First
Demographic questions are the least interesting and should be placed at the end of the survey. Starting with interesting questions motivates respondents to continue. Sensitive or potentially objectionable questions should also be near the end once respondents are engaged.

### Error 12: Not Separating Quantitative and Qualitative Analysis
Survey data analysis should separate quantitative and qualitative data. Quantitative data is analyzed with descriptive and inferential statistics. Qualitative data from open-ended questions requires content analysis. Treating them the same leads to inappropriate analysis methods.

### Error 13: Ignoring Nonresponse Bias
Failing to consider differences between respondents and non-respondents can lead to biased findings. If the sampling frame is split evenly by gender but 90% of responses are from males, there is significant nonresponse bias. Multiple contacts and incentives can help reduce this bias.

### Error 14: Using Automated Translation for Multilingual Surveys
When surveys need to be in multiple languages, professional human translation is necessary. Automated tools are not sufficient for ensuring that surveys in different languages are actually asking the same questions without mistranslations.

### Error 15: Not Considering Mode Effects
Different survey modes (paper, online, telephone) may produce different response patterns. Researchers should consider how mode might affect their data and ensure that mixed-mode surveys don't introduce biases from instruments that have minor differences.

### Error 16: Over-Relying on Significance Testing for Nonprobability Samples
Statistical significance tests assume random sampling. For nonprobability samples, significance levels may not be meaningful. Researchers should focus on descriptive statistics, effect sizes, and practical significance rather than relying solely on p-values.

### Error 17: Not Validating Survey Items
Failing to evaluate survey items through techniques like exploratory factor analysis can result in including items that don't measure the intended constructs. Items with low factor loadings or high cross-loadings should be reviewed and potentially dropped to improve survey quality.

### Error 18: Including Pilot Study Participants in Main Study
Individuals who participated in the pilot study should generally not take part in the main study. Their participation could bias future responses, and including their data would compromise the integrity of the main data collection.
