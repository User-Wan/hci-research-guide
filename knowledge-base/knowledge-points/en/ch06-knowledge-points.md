# Chapter 6: Diaries — Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

### Diary Study
A diary study is a longitudinal research method in which participants self-report their activities, experiences, or thoughts over a period of time by recording entries in a diary or journal. The diary is a self-report instrument that users fill out themselves, typically in their natural settings. Diary studies bridge the gap between one-time surveys and resource-intensive field observations, enabling researchers to collect data about behaviors and experiences that occur outside the laboratory, over extended timeframes, and in ecologically valid contexts.

### Feedback Diary
A feedback diary is a type of diary study instrument where participants report on specific events, experiences, or reactions as they occur. The participant is typically given a structured or semi-structured form to complete each time a particular event of interest takes place. For example, a participant might fill out an entry every time they experience frustration with a computer system. Feedback diaries are event-contingent — the recording trigger is the occurrence of the event itself. They are well-suited for capturing experiences related to specific phenomena such as user frustration, errors, or particular types of tasks.

### Elicitation Diary
An elicitation diary is a diary method where participants collect artifacts, create representations, or produce creative outputs as part of their diary entries, which are later used to prompt discussion and deeper reflection, often in follow-up interviews. Rather than simply reporting on events, participants might take photographs, collect physical objects, sketch diagrams, or write narratives that serve as "probes" to elicit richer data during subsequent conversations. Elicitation diaries are particularly valuable for inspiring design and understanding user experiences in ways that purely text-based reporting cannot achieve. An example is the FETCH system, where participants collected items and later used their collections to guide interview discussions.

### Hybrid Diary
A hybrid diary combines elements of both feedback and elicitation approaches within a single study. Participants may both report on specific events (feedback component) and collect artifacts or produce creative outputs (elicitation component). This approach maximizes the breadth and depth of data collected, allowing researchers to capture both structured event data and rich, contextual material for later exploration.

### Time Diary
A time diary is a structured diary format where participants record all of their activities (or a specific category of activities) over designated time periods, typically providing a continuous chronological account. Time diaries are useful for understanding how people allocate their time, what activities co-occur, and how behaviors are sequenced. They differ from feedback diaries in that the recording trigger is time-based (e.g., every hour, at the end of each day) rather than event-based.

### Recall Bias
Recall bias is the systematic error that occurs when participants cannot accurately remember or reconstruct past events, activities, or experiences. It is one of the central challenges in diary studies and self-report research more broadly. The longer the delay between an event and the recording of that event, the greater the potential for recall bias. Diary studies are specifically designed to mitigate recall bias by having participants record events close to the time they occur, rather than relying on retrospective accounts gathered in a single interview or survey.

### Naturalistic Setting
A naturalistic setting refers to the real-world environment in which participants normally carry out their activities, as opposed to a controlled laboratory environment. Diary studies are conducted in naturalistic settings, which enhances ecological validity — the degree to which research findings reflect real-world conditions. Participants record their entries in their homes, workplaces, schools, or wherever their daily activities take place.

### Ecological Validity
Ecological validity is the extent to which the findings of a research study accurately reflect real-world behaviors and experiences. Diary studies enhance ecological validity because data is collected in participants' natural environments, during their actual activities, and over realistic time periods. This stands in contrast to laboratory studies, where the artificial setting may influence behavior.

### Snowball Sampling
Snowball sampling is a non-probability sampling technique in which existing study participants recruit future participants from among their acquaintances. This method is particularly useful for reaching populations that are difficult to identify or access through conventional sampling methods. In diary research, snowball sampling has been used to recruit participants from specific communities, such as blind computer users or open source software contributors, where participants can identify others who share relevant characteristics.

### Eureka Moments
Eureka moments (also called "aha moments") are instances of sudden insight, discovery, or realization that occur during the use of a product or system. In diary studies, researchers have used elicitation diaries to capture these moments of sudden understanding or breakthrough, which are valuable for understanding the learning process and identifying key features or experiences that drive user satisfaction and engagement.

### Self-Report Instrument
A self-report instrument is any research tool (questionnaire, diary, survey) in which participants provide information about their own behaviors, experiences, attitudes, or characteristics. Diaries are a form of self-report instrument, meaning the quality of the data depends on participants' willingness and ability to accurately report their experiences.

### Content Analysis
Content analysis is a systematic method for analyzing qualitative data by categorizing and coding textual or visual material into themes, patterns, or categories. In diary studies, content analysis is used to analyze diary entries, identifying recurring themes, patterns of behavior, and significant events across participants and time periods. Researchers develop coding schemes, identify themes, and may use multiple coders with interrater reliability checks.

### Interrater Reliability
Interrater reliability is the degree of agreement among multiple coders or raters when independently categorizing or coding the same data. In diary study analysis, when multiple researchers code diary entries, interrater reliability metrics (such as Cohen's kappa or percentage agreement) are used to ensure that the coding scheme is applied consistently and that the analysis is trustworthy.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

### User Frustration Research Framework
The user frustration research framework is an approach to studying negative emotional experiences in human-computer interaction through systematic self-reporting. Pioneered by researchers such as Lazar, Jones, Bessiere, and Ceaparu, this framework uses diary studies to capture instances of computer frustration as they occur in naturalistic settings. The framework recognizes that frustration is one of the most common negative experiences in computing, and that studying it requires capturing data in the moment of occurrence rather than through retrospective accounts. Large-scale implementations of this framework have included studies with 111 university students, 50 workplace professionals, and 100 blind users, revealing patterns such as the average time lost to frustration (approximately 42% of total computing time in some contexts) and the most common causes of frustration (lost work, time wasted, and help system failures).

### Naturalistic Settings Methodology
Naturalistic settings methodology is the theoretical stance that research conducted in participants' real-world environments yields more valid and generalizable findings than research conducted in artificial laboratory settings. For diary studies, this framework provides the justification for having participants record their entries in their homes, workplaces, or other natural environments. The methodology emphasizes that the context in which technology is used fundamentally shapes the user experience, and that removing users from their natural context (as in a lab study) may alter the behaviors and experiences being studied. This is closely related to the concept of ecological validity.

### Sociology Diary Methodology
Sociology diary methodology refers to the established use of diary methods in sociological research, from which HCI researchers have adapted diary study techniques. Sociologists have long used diaries to study time use, household labor, media consumption, and other social phenomena. This body of methodological work provides guidance on diary design, participant training, data quality assurance, and analysis techniques that HCI researchers can draw upon. Key contributions from sociology include time-use diaries, the distinction between event-contingent and signal-contingent recording, and techniques for ensuring compliance and data completeness.

### Longitudinal Research Design
Longitudinal research design is the theoretical framework for studying phenomena over extended periods of time, collecting data at multiple time points to capture change, development, and patterns. Diary studies are inherently longitudinal, spanning days, weeks, or even months. This framework addresses issues such as participant attrition, practice effects, fatigue, and the challenge of maintaining data quality over time. It also provides guidance on determining appropriate study duration, sampling intervals, and methods for analyzing temporal patterns.

### Probe-Based Research Methodology
Probe-based research methodology is an approach in which researchers provide participants with provocative or evocative materials (probes) designed to prompt reflection, self-report, and dialogue. In diary research, probes may include cameras, artifact collection bags, creative prompts, or structured recording templates. The probes serve as intermediaries between the researcher and participant, enabling the collection of data that would be difficult to obtain through direct observation or questioning alone. This methodology is particularly associated with cultural probes and technology probes.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Define Research Questions and Objectives
Before designing the diary study, clearly articulate the research questions and objectives. Determine what behaviors, experiences, or phenomena you want to understand. The research questions will guide all subsequent decisions about diary type, format, duration, and participant selection. Consider whether your questions are better suited to feedback reporting (event-based), artifact elicitation (collection-based), or time-based recording.

### Step 2: Select the Diary Type
Based on the research questions, choose the most appropriate diary type: feedback diary, elicitation diary, hybrid diary, or time diary. Feedback diaries are best when you need to capture specific events or reactions. Elicitation diaries are best when you want rich, contextual data to inspire design or explore experiences deeply. Hybrid diaries combine both approaches. Time diaries are best for understanding activity patterns and time allocation. Consider the cognitive and time burden on participants when making this choice.

### Step 3: Design the Diary Instrument
Design the recording instrument that participants will use. For feedback diaries, create structured or semi-structured forms with specific fields (date, time, activity, event description, emotional response, severity, etc.). For elicitation diaries, provide clear instructions and materials for artifact collection or creative expression. Consider the recording medium — paper diaries, digital forms, audio recordings, photographs, or specialized diary applications. Ensure that the instrument is neither too burdensome (leading to non-compliance) nor too sparse (leading to insufficient data).

### Step 4: Recruit and Select Participants
Identify and recruit participants who are appropriate for the research questions. Use purposeful sampling to ensure that participants are likely to experience the phenomena of interest. Consider snowball sampling for hard-to-reach populations. Determine the number of participants needed — diary studies have ranged from as few as 11 users to as many as 300+ in published research. Provide clear information about the study's purpose, duration, expectations, and compensation. Avoid linking compensation directly to the number of entries, as this may incentivize low-quality entries.

### Step 5: Define Recording Triggers and Time Frames
Establish when participants should make diary entries. For event-contingent diaries (feedback diaries), clearly define what constitutes a recordable event. For signal-contingent diaries, determine the sampling schedule (e.g., every hour, three times daily). For time diaries, specify the time periods to be covered. Determine the overall study duration, which typically ranges from one to four weeks but can extend longer. Shorter durations may not capture enough events; longer durations increase participant fatigue and attrition.

### Step 6: Conduct Pre-Study Training
Before the diary period begins, meet with participants to explain the study procedures, demonstrate the diary instrument, and answer questions. Provide written instructions as a reference. For elicitation diaries, show examples of the types of artifacts or materials to collect. Conduct a practice session if possible. This training is crucial for ensuring that participants understand what is expected and can produce high-quality entries.

### Step 7: Monitor Data Collection During the Study
During the diary period, maintain contact with participants to monitor compliance, answer questions, and address problems. Some studies use check-in calls or messages at predetermined intervals. Monitoring helps identify participants who are struggling with the diary format or who may be about to drop out. However, avoid being so intrusive that the monitoring itself becomes a burden or alters participants' natural behavior.

### Step 8: Conduct Post-Study Interviews or Debriefings
After the diary period, conduct follow-up interviews or debriefing sessions with participants. These sessions serve multiple purposes: clarifying ambiguous entries, probing deeper into interesting or unusual events, gathering retrospective reflections on the diary experience, and collecting additional contextual information. For elicitation diaries, the post-study interview is where the collected artifacts serve as prompts for rich discussion. These interviews are typically semi-structured, allowing researchers to follow up on specific entries while also exploring emergent themes.

### Step 9: Analyze Diary Data
Analyze the collected diary entries using appropriate qualitative or quantitative methods. Common approaches include content analysis (coding entries into themes and categories), statistical analysis of event frequencies and patterns, temporal analysis of when events occur, and cross-case comparison across participants. When multiple coders are involved, establish interrater reliability. Combine quantitative counts and distributions with qualitative analysis of the content and context of entries. Consider using the diary entries in conjunction with interview data for a more complete analysis.

### Step 10: Synthesize Findings and Relate to Research Questions
Synthesize the analysis results to address the original research questions. Identify key patterns, themes, and insights that emerge across participants and time. Relate findings to existing literature and theoretical frameworks. Consider the limitations of the diary data (e.g., potential for underreporting, self-selection bias) and triangulate with other data sources where possible. Present findings in a way that captures both the breadth of the diary data (patterns across many entries and participants) and the depth (rich individual experiences and stories).

## 4. Decision Criteria

### When to Use Feedback Diaries vs. Elicitation Diaries
Use feedback diaries when the research goal is to systematically capture specific events, reactions, or experiences as they occur. Feedback diaries are ideal for studying phenomena that can be clearly defined in advance, such as user frustration events, error occurrences, or particular task types. Use elicitation diaries when the research goal is to gather rich, contextual, and creative data that can inspire design or provide deep understanding of user experiences. Elicitation diaries are ideal for exploratory research where the phenomena of interest are not yet well-defined and where visual, spatial, or artifact-based data would be valuable. Consider hybrid diaries when both structured event data and rich contextual data are needed.

### When to Use Diaries vs. Surveys vs. Observation
Use diaries when you need longitudinal data collected close to the time of events, in naturalistic settings, with a level of detail and context that surveys cannot provide. Use surveys when you need quick, one-time data from a large sample about attitudes, preferences, or self-reported behaviors, and when temporal proximity to events is not critical. Use observation (field observation or ethnography) when you need unbiased, detailed behavioral data and have the resources to be present in participants' environments over extended periods. Diaries occupy a middle ground: they provide temporal proximity and naturalistic context (like observation) while being less resource-intensive and more scalable (like surveys).

### When to Use Time Diaries
Use time diaries when the research goal is to understand how participants allocate their time across activities, what activities co-occur, how daily routines are structured, or how much time is spent on particular tasks or technologies. Time diaries are especially valuable when studying work patterns, media use, or daily routines where the temporal structure of activities is itself of interest.

### When to Use Structured vs. Unstructured Diary Formats
Use structured diary formats (with predefined fields, checkboxes, and scales) when the phenomena of interest are well-understood and you want to ensure consistency across participants and entries, facilitating quantitative analysis. Use unstructured or semi-structured formats (with open-ended prompts, narrative space, or creative freedom) when the research is exploratory, the phenomena are not well-categorized in advance, or when you want to capture the richness and diversity of participants' experiences without constraining their responses. Consider the participant population — structured formats may be easier for participants who are less experienced with self-reflection or writing.

### When to Use Paper vs. Digital Diary Instruments
Use paper diaries when participants may not have reliable access to technology, when the study involves artifact collection or physical materials, or when a low-tech approach reduces barriers to participation. Use digital diaries (apps, web forms, mobile notifications) when timestamping accuracy is important, when real-time monitoring of compliance is needed, when participants are comfortable with technology, or when features like photo upload, audio recording, or automated reminders would enhance data quality. Digital diaries can automatically capture metadata (time, location) and send reminders, but may be less flexible for creative or artifact-based entries.

### How Many Participants to Recruit
The number of participants depends on the research questions, the diary type, the study duration, and the desired depth of analysis. Diary studies have ranged from as few as 11 participants (task switching study) to over 300 (large-scale frustration studies across multiple populations). For qualitative diary studies focused on rich, detailed analysis, 10–30 participants may be sufficient. For studies aiming to identify patterns across a broader population or to make quantitative claims, larger samples are needed. Consider that diary studies have higher attrition rates than one-time studies, so recruit more participants than the minimum needed.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

### Case 1: User Frustration Studies (Lazar, Jones, Bessiere, Ceaparu)
This series of large-scale diary studies examined computer frustration across diverse populations. The first study involved 111 university students who kept diaries of frustrating computer experiences over a semester. A second study replicated the approach with 50 workplace professionals. A third study adapted the methodology for 100 blind computer users, using audio-recorded diary entries instead of written ones. Findings revealed that frustration is pervasive: the average user lost approximately 42% of the time they spent on a computer to frustrating experiences. The most common sources of frustration included lost work, time wasted on unhelpful help systems, and software crashes. The blind user study revealed additional sources of frustration specific to assistive technology use, such as screen reader incompatibility with web pages. The diary instrument was a simple form completed each time frustration occurred, capturing the application being used, the cause of frustration, the duration, and the emotional response.

### Case 2: Password Usage Diary Study (Adams & Sasse)
This diary study investigated how people actually use passwords in their daily lives, challenging the assumption that security guidelines are practical and followed. Twenty participants kept diaries of all password-related events over a period of time, generating approximately 1,500 events in total. Participants recorded each time they needed to enter a password, create a password, encounter an authentication failure, or take any password-related action. The diary data revealed that the average user had far more passwords than security experts assumed, that users frequently forgot passwords, and that users developed personal strategies (such as writing passwords down or using the same password across systems) that contradicted official security recommendations. This research provided empirical evidence that password security policies need to account for real-world user behavior.

### Case 3: FETCH System — Item Tracking with Elicitation Diaries
The FETCH system study used an elicitation diary approach in which participants collected items of interest over time. Participants gathered physical artifacts, web clippings, notes, and other materials that represented their information-seeking activities. These collections were then used as prompts in follow-up interviews, where participants explained the context and significance of each item. This elicitation approach revealed rich information about how people discover, evaluate, and organize information in their daily lives — insights that would not have been captured by a simple feedback diary reporting on search events.

### Case 4: Task Switching Diary Study (11 Users)
This focused diary study examined how 11 users switched between tasks and applications during their daily computer use over a two-week period. Participants recorded each instance of switching from one application to another, including the reason for the switch, the time spent in each application, and whether the switch was voluntary or involuntary (e.g., prompted by a notification). The small sample size allowed for deep, detailed analysis of each participant's switching patterns. Findings revealed the high frequency of task switching, the role of interruptions in triggering switches, and the cognitive costs associated with returning to interrupted tasks.

### Case 5: Rendezvous Study (34 Participants)
This diary study examined how 34 participants coordinated and managed social rendezvous — planned meetings with friends, colleagues, or acquaintances — over a multi-week period. Participants recorded each rendezvous-related event, including planning activities, communication with others, travel, and the meeting itself. The diary data revealed the complexity of social coordination, the variety of communication channels used (phone, text, email, in-person), and the challenges of managing schedules across multiple participants. The study provided design insights for location-aware and social coordination technologies.

### Case 6: Open Source Contributor Motivation Study
This diary study used an elicitation approach to understand what motivated contributors to participate in open source software projects. Contributors kept diaries documenting their contributions, motivations, frustrations, and experiences over a period of time. The diary data, combined with follow-up interviews, revealed a complex mix of motivations including intrinsic enjoyment, reputation building, skill development, and community belonging. The elicitation approach was particularly valuable for capturing the emotional and social dimensions of participation that quantitative surveys had missed.

### Case 7: Eureka Moments Study
This diary study used an elicitation diary approach to capture instances of sudden insight or understanding ("eureka moments") as participants learned to use new software or discovered new features in familiar software. Participants recorded moments when they suddenly understood how something worked, discovered a useful feature, or had a breakthrough in their understanding. The collected entries were used in follow-up interviews to explore the conditions that enabled these moments. Findings informed the design of help systems, tutorials, and onboarding experiences.

### Case 8: Work-Related Reading Diary Study
This diary study examined how knowledge workers engaged with work-related reading materials (documents, emails, reports, web pages) over a period of time. Participants recorded each reading event, including the material being read, the purpose of reading, the duration, and the context (at desk, on commute, etc.). The study used structured time-based sampling to ensure comprehensive coverage of reading activities. Findings revealed patterns in how reading was integrated with other work activities and how workers prioritized and managed their reading.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

### Error 1: Underestimating Recall Bias
One of the most common errors in diary studies is not adequately addressing recall bias. If participants wait too long after an event to record it, their account may be inaccurate or incomplete. To mitigate this, design the diary instrument to encourage recording as close to the time of the event as possible. Use reminders, simple entry formats, and mobile recording tools. The longer the delay between event and recording, the less reliable the data.

### Error 2: Creating Excessively Burdensome Diary Instruments
If the diary instrument is too long, too complex, or requires too much effort for each entry, participants will either stop making entries, make increasingly brief and low-quality entries, or drop out of the study entirely. Design instruments that capture the essential information with minimal effort. Pilot test the instrument with a few participants before launching the full study. A short, consistently completed diary is more valuable than a long, inconsistently completed one.

### Error 3: Linking Compensation to Entry Count
Paying participants per diary entry creates a perverse incentive to maximize the number of entries at the expense of quality. Participants may fabricate entries, duplicate similar entries, or make trivial entries to increase their compensation. Instead, compensate participants for their overall participation in the study, and use monitoring and follow-up interviews to ensure data quality.

### Error 4: Failing to Define Key Terms and Recording Triggers
If participants do not clearly understand what constitutes a "recordable event," the diary data will be inconsistent. Some participants may over-report (recording trivial events) while others may under-report (missing significant events). Provide clear, concrete definitions and examples of what should be recorded. For frustration studies, for example, define what level of negative experience qualifies as "frustration" versus mere "annoyance."

### Error 5: Insufficient Monitoring During the Study Period
Diary studies that do not include regular check-ins with participants during the data collection period often suffer from declining compliance over time. Participants may forget to make entries, lose motivation, or encounter problems with the recording instrument. Schedule periodic check-ins (every few days to weekly) to maintain engagement, answer questions, and identify problems early. Automated reminders in digital diary systems can also help.

### Error 6: Not Combining Diaries with Other Methods
Relying solely on diary data without triangulating with other methods (interviews, observation, system logs) limits the richness and validity of findings. Diary entries capture the participant's perspective but may miss contextual details, nonverbal cues, or behaviors that participants do not think to report. Post-study interviews are particularly important for probing diary entries, clarifying ambiguities, and gathering additional context. Consider combining diaries with interviews, observation, or log analysis.

### Error 7: Insufficient Pilot Testing
Launching a full diary study without pilot testing the instrument, procedures, and analysis plan is risky. Pilot testing with a small number of participants (3–5) can reveal problems with unclear instructions, confusing question formats, missing response options, or impractical recording requirements. It also helps estimate the time required per entry and the expected rate of event occurrence, informing decisions about study duration and sample size.

### Error 8: Ignoring Participant Dropout and Attrition
Diary studies, especially long ones, inevitably experience participant dropout. Failing to account for this in the study design can leave the researcher with insufficient data. Over-recruit participants (e.g., by 20–30%) to compensate for expected attrition. Monitor compliance during the study and have strategies in place to support struggling participants. Analyze whether dropouts differ systematically from completers, as this may introduce bias.

### Error 9: Poor Data Management and Organization
Diary studies generate large volumes of data, especially when multiple participants record entries over weeks. Without a systematic approach to organizing, storing, and managing this data, researchers can lose track of entries, mix up participants, or struggle to retrieve specific data during analysis. Establish a data management plan before the study begins, including naming conventions, storage systems, backup procedures, and data cleaning protocols.

### Error 10: Overgeneralizing from Limited Samples
Diary study participants are often a self-selected group who are willing to commit to the effort of regular self-reporting. This group may not be representative of the broader population of interest. Be cautious about generalizing findings beyond the study sample. Use purposeful sampling to ensure some degree of representativeness, and acknowledge the limitations of the sample when reporting findings. Large-scale studies with diverse participants (like the user frustration studies with students, workers, and blind users) provide stronger grounds for generalization.
