# Chapter 7: Case Studies — Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

### Case Study
A case study is an empirical inquiry that investigates a contemporary phenomenon (the "case") in depth and within its real-world context, especially when the boundaries between phenomenon and context may not be clearly evident. In HCI, case studies involve the detailed examination of a particular individual, group, organization, event, or system as it naturally occurs. Case studies use multiple sources of evidence and benefit from prior development of theoretical propositions to guide data collection and analysis. They are particularly valuable for exploring complex, context-dependent phenomena that cannot be meaningfully studied outside of their natural setting.

### Intrinsic Case Study
An intrinsic case study is one undertaken because the researcher has a genuine interest in the particular case itself, not because the case represents a broader class of phenomena or is being used to build theory. The case is intrinsically interesting in its own right. In HCI, an intrinsic case study might focus on a specific individual's unique use of assistive technology (such as Sara's use of a Braille display and screen reader) because that particular person's experience is inherently worthy of understanding. The goal is to understand the case in its full particularity, not to generalize beyond it.

### Instrumental Case Study
An instrumental case study is one in which a particular case is examined primarily to provide insight into a broader issue or to refine a general theory. The case itself is of secondary interest; it plays a supportive role, facilitating understanding of something else. In HCI, an instrumental case study might examine a single organization's adoption of a new collaborative technology to understand broader patterns of technology adoption in workplaces. Multiple instrumental cases can be combined to create a collective case study that strengthens theoretical generalizations.

### Single Case Study
A single case study involves the in-depth investigation of one case — a single individual, group, organization, or event. Single cases are appropriate when the case is a critical test of existing theory, when the case is rare or unique (extreme or unusual case), or when the case is revelatory (the investigator has access to a phenomenon previously inaccessible to scientific inquiry). The case of Sara, a blind college student using assistive technology, is an example of a single case study in HCI.

### Multiple Case Study (Collective Case Study)
A multiple case study (also called a collective case study) involves the study of several cases, either simultaneously or sequentially, to investigate a phenomenon. Each individual case is studied in its own context, and then findings are compared across cases. Multiple case designs are considered more robust than single case designs for theory building because they allow for literal replication (predicting similar results across cases) and theoretical replication (predicting contrasting results for predictable reasons). A study of assistive technology use across multiple users with different disabilities would be a multiple case study.

### Embedded Case Study
An embedded case study is one in which multiple units of analysis are studied within a single case. For example, a case study of a company's technology adoption (the case) might examine individual employees (embedded units), departments (embedded units), and the overall organization. The embedded design ensures that the study covers more than just the global nature of the case, attending to specific sub-units. The International Children's Digital Library study, which examined children in four countries with individual children as embedded units, is an example of an embedded design.

### Holistic Case Study
A holistic case study is one in which the case is analyzed as a whole, without attempting to identify or examine specific embedded units of analysis within it. A holistic design is appropriate when no logical sub-units can be identified or when the relevant theory underlying the case study is itself holistic. However, holistic designs risk the risk that the study becomes superficial, failing to examine specific phenomena in sufficient depth.

### Unit of Analysis
The unit of analysis defines what constitutes the "case" in a case study — the entity about which data is collected and conclusions are drawn. Units of analysis can be individuals, groups, organizations, events, projects, technologies, or even countries. Defining the unit of analysis is a critical early decision in case study design. In the International Children's Digital Library study, the unit of analysis was the child participant; in a study of a company's technology adoption, the unit might be the organization or the team.

### Literal Replication
Literal replication is a multiple case study strategy in which cases are selected because they are predicted to produce similar results for predictable reasons. If the theory predicts that a particular phenomenon will occur under certain conditions, literal replication tests that prediction by selecting multiple cases that meet those conditions. For example, if a theory predicts that blind users will experience certain frustrations with screen readers, literal replication would involve studying multiple blind users to confirm that the predicted frustrations occur.

### Theoretical Replication
Theoretical replication is a multiple case study strategy in which cases are selected because they are predicted to produce contrasting results for predictable reasons. This strengthens the theory by showing that it can account for variation. For example, if a theory predicts that technology adoption differs between small and large organizations, theoretical replication would involve studying both a small and a large organization to confirm that the predicted differences emerge.

### Data Triangulation
Data triangulation is the use of multiple data sources, methods, or investigators to study a single phenomenon, increasing the validity and credibility of findings. In case studies, triangulation typically involves combining data from interviews, observations, document analysis, and artifact examination. When findings from different sources converge, confidence in the findings increases. When they diverge, this signals the need for further investigation. Triangulation is a hallmark of rigorous case study research.

### Pattern Matching
Pattern matching is a case study analysis technique in which empirically observed patterns are compared against predicted patterns derived from theory or prior research. If the observed pattern matches the predicted pattern, this strengthens the validity of the findings and the theory. If the patterns diverge, the researcher must consider alternative explanations or revise the theory. Pattern matching can involve literal replication (same pattern across cases) and theoretical replication (different but theoretically predicted patterns across cases).

### External Validity
External validity refers to the degree to which findings from a study can be generalized beyond the specific cases studied. In case studies, external validity is a common concern because the small number of cases and the contextual specificity of findings may limit generalizability. However, case studies achieve analytic generalization (generalizing to theory) rather than statistical generalization (generalizing to populations). The goal is to expand and generalize theories, not to enumerate frequencies.

### Technology Biography
A technology biography is an in-depth narrative account of an individual's history and experience with technology over time. It is a data collection approach used in case studies that examines how a person came to use particular technologies, how their use has evolved, and what role technology plays in their life. Technology biographies provide rich, contextual understanding of the relationship between a person and their technological environment. The detailed account of Sara's assistive technology use is an example of a technology biography.

### Critical Case
A critical case is a case selected because it is predicted to be a clear, decisive case that will confirm or disconfirm a particular theory or proposition. Critical cases are chosen for their strategic value in theory testing. A case that meets all the conditions specified by a theory but does not produce the predicted outcome would strongly challenge the theory.

### Extreme Case
An extreme case is a case selected because it is unusually dramatic, unusual, or extreme in some way that makes it particularly informative. Extreme cases may reveal phenomena that are difficult to observe in more typical cases. For example, studying a person with severe disabilities who has developed innovative technology workarounds may reveal design principles that are applicable more broadly.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

### Qualitative Research Methodology
Case studies are a core method within qualitative research methodology, which emphasizes understanding phenomena from the perspectives of participants, in their natural contexts, through rich and detailed data. Qualitative methodology values depth over breadth, context-sensitivity over control, and meaning-making over measurement. Within this tradition, case studies are distinguished from other qualitative methods (such as ethnography, grounded theory, or phenomenology) by their focus on a bounded system (the case) and their use of multiple data sources. This methodology provides the philosophical foundation for case study research, including assumptions about the nature of knowledge (epistemology), the nature of reality (ontology), and the role of the researcher.

### Case Study Research Design Framework (Yin)
Robert Yin's framework for case study research provides a systematic approach to designing, conducting, and reporting case studies. The framework specifies that a case study design should include: (1) the study's questions (typically "how" or "why" questions about contemporary phenomena), (2) its propositions (theoretical statements guiding data collection), (3) its unit of analysis (what constitutes the case), (4) the logic linking the data to the propositions, and (5) the criteria for interpreting the findings. This framework emphasizes the importance of a formal research protocol, theoretical replication in multiple case designs, and the use of multiple sources of evidence with triangulation. It provides a rigorous structure that addresses common criticisms of case study research (such as lack of rigor, bias, and limited generalizability).

### Contextual Examination Model
The contextual examination model is the theoretical basis for studying phenomena within their real-world contexts rather than in isolation. This model recognizes that human behavior, technology use, and organizational processes are deeply embedded in social, cultural, organizational, and physical contexts that shape and are shaped by the phenomena under study. In HCI case studies, this model justifies the in-depth examination of technology use within the specific settings where it occurs — homes, workplaces, schools, hospitals — rather than in artificial laboratory environments. The model argues that meaningful understanding of human-technology interaction requires attending to the contextual factors that influence it.

### Data Source Integration Framework
The data source integration framework guides the systematic combination of multiple data sources in case study research. This framework specifies how data from interviews, observations, documents, artifacts, and other sources should be collected, organized, and integrated to produce coherent and well-supported findings. The framework emphasizes triangulation (cross-checking findings across sources), complementarity (using different sources to illuminate different facets of the phenomenon), and integration (combining sources to produce a more complete picture than any single source could provide). It addresses the practical challenges of managing diverse data types and ensuring that the analysis draws on all available evidence.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Define the Research Questions
Begin by formulating clear, focused research questions. Case study questions are typically "how" or "why" questions that seek to understand a phenomenon in its real-world context (e.g., "How do blind college students adapt assistive technology to their academic needs?" or "Why do some technology adoption efforts succeed while others fail?"). The questions should be broad enough to allow for unexpected findings but specific enough to guide data collection. Avoid questions that are better addressed by surveys (what, how many) or experiments (does X cause Y).

### Step 2: Develop Theoretical Propositions
Develop theoretical propositions or a conceptual framework that guides the case study design. Propositions are theoretical statements derived from the literature, prior research, or professional experience that suggest what you expect to find and why. These propositions guide data collection by focusing attention on specific aspects of the case and provide a basis for interpreting the findings. For example, a proposition might state that "users with visual impairments develop workarounds for inaccessible software that reflect both their technical skills and their social support networks."

### Step 3: Select the Case(s) and Define the Unit of Analysis
Select the case or cases to be studied and define the unit of analysis. The case may be an individual, group, organization, event, or technology. The selection should be purposeful, guided by the research questions and theoretical propositions. For single case studies, select a case that is critical, extreme, unique, or revelatory. For multiple case studies, select cases that allow for literal replication (similar cases) or theoretical replication (contrasting cases). Define the boundaries of the case — what is included and excluded — to ensure a manageable scope.

### Step 4: Develop the Case Study Protocol
Develop a formal case study protocol that outlines the procedures for data collection, the instruments to be used, and the rules to be followed. The protocol should include: an overview of the case study project, field procedures (access arrangements, contact information, general procedural guidelines), and the case study questions (the specific questions that the data must address). The protocol serves as a guide for the researcher and a tool for ensuring consistency and rigor, especially in multiple case studies or studies involving multiple researchers.

### Step 5: Identify and Prepare Data Sources
Identify the data sources to be used in the case study. Common sources in HCI case studies include: interviews with participants and stakeholders, direct observation of technology use, documents (emails, reports, manuals, design documents), artifacts (the technology itself, prototypes, physical objects), archival records (system logs, usage data), and audio-visual materials (photographs, videos). Prepare the data collection instruments (interview guides, observation protocols, document analysis templates). Ensure that you have access to all necessary data sources.

### Step 6: Conduct Pilot Testing
Pilot test the case study protocol and instruments with a preliminary case or a subset of the data collection activities. Pilot testing helps refine the data collection procedures, identify problems with interview questions or observation protocols, and develop the initial coding scheme for analysis. The pilot case is not necessarily part of the final study, but its data may be included if appropriate. Pilot testing is especially important for multiple case studies, where consistency across cases is essential.

### Step 7: Collect Data from Multiple Sources
Collect data from the sources identified in Step 5, following the case study protocol. Conduct interviews, make observations, gather documents and artifacts, and record data systematically. Maintain a case study database — an organized collection of all data, notes, and materials — to ensure that the evidence is accessible and auditable. In multiple case studies, complete data collection for each case before moving to the next, allowing the analysis of each case to inform the collection of subsequent cases (replication logic).

### Step 8: Analyze the Case Data
Analyze the collected data using appropriate analytical techniques. Common approaches in case study research include: pattern matching (comparing observed patterns to predicted patterns), explanation building (developing a narrative explanation of the case), time-series analysis (examining changes over time), and logic models (tracing chains of events or processes). Use the theoretical propositions as a starting framework, but remain open to unexpected patterns and emergent themes. Triangulate across data sources to strengthen the analysis. In multiple case studies, conduct within-case analysis (analyzing each case individually) before cross-case analysis (comparing patterns across cases).

### Step 9: Address Alternative Explanations and Rival Interpretations
Systematically consider alternative explanations for the findings. For every major finding or conclusion, ask: "What else could explain this?" and actively seek evidence that might support rival interpretations. This process strengthens the validity of the findings by ruling out or accounting for alternative explanations. In multiple case studies, theoretical replication logic helps address rival explanations by showing that contrasting cases produce the predicted different outcomes.

### Step 10: Write Up the Case Study
Compose the case study report, presenting the findings in a clear, compelling, and well-structured narrative. The write-up should include: an introduction with the research questions and significance, a description of the case (and its context), the methodology, the findings (organized thematically or chronologically), and the conclusions (including implications for theory, design, and practice). For multiple case studies, the report typically includes individual case descriptions followed by a cross-case analysis. Use rich, detailed quotations and descriptions to convey the case in its full context. Address the limitations of the study, including potential threats to validity.

## 4. Decision Criteria

### When to Use Single vs. Multiple Case Studies
Use a single case study when the case is a critical test of a well-formulated theory, when the case is rare or unique (an extreme or unusual case that is unlikely to be replicated), when the case is revelatory (the researcher has access to a phenomenon that has not been previously studied), or when the case is a longitudinal study of the same case at different points in time. Use multiple case studies when the research goal is to develop or test theory through replication, when greater confidence in the findings is needed, or when the phenomenon varies across contexts in theoretically meaningful ways. Multiple case designs are more compelling and robust but require significantly more resources.

### When to Use Embedded vs. Holistic Case Designs
Use an embedded design when the research questions require examining multiple units of analysis within the case (e.g., individual users within a team, or teams within an organization). Embedded designs ensure that the study attends to specific sub-units rather than only the global nature of the case. Use a holistic design when the theory underlying the study is itself holistic (e.g., studying an organization's culture as a whole), when no logical sub-units can be identified, or when the scope of the study precludes detailed analysis of sub-units. Be cautious with holistic designs, as they risk superficiality.

### When to Use Formal vs. Informal Case Studies
Use formal case studies (with a defined protocol, systematic data collection, and rigorous analysis) when the research goal is theory building or testing, when the findings will be submitted for academic publication, or when the rigor of the study must withstand critical scrutiny. Use informal case studies (with less structured data collection and analysis) when the goal is preliminary exploration, design inspiration, or practitioner learning, or when resources are insufficient for a full formal study. Informal case studies, such as the "war stories" that designers share, can be valuable for generating hypotheses and inspiring design but should not be mistaken for rigorous research.

### When to Use an Intrinsic vs. Instrumental Approach
Use an intrinsic approach when the case itself is of primary interest — when the goal is to understand this particular person, group, or situation in its full complexity and particularity. This is appropriate when the case has inherent value (e.g., a remarkable individual, a unique situation). Use an instrumental approach when the case is being studied to provide insight into a broader issue — when the case serves as a vehicle for understanding a general phenomenon. The choice between intrinsic and instrumental approaches determines the extent to which findings are intended to generalize beyond the case.

### How to Select Cases for Multiple Case Studies
Select cases using replication logic, not sampling logic. In replication logic, cases are chosen because they are predicted to produce either similar results (literal replication) or contrasting results for predictable reasons (theoretical replication). A minimum of two cases for literal replication and two cases for theoretical replication is recommended. Avoid selecting cases based on convenience or random sampling — case study generalization is to theory, not to populations. Consider practical factors such as access, feasibility, and the richness of data likely available from each case.

### How Many Data Sources to Use
Use at least two independent sources of evidence for every major finding or conclusion. This minimum allows for triangulation and strengthens the validity of the findings. More sources provide greater confidence and richer understanding. Common combinations include interviews + observations, interviews + documents, or observations + artifacts + documents. The choice of data sources should be guided by the research questions and the nature of the case.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

### Case 1: Sara's Assistive Technology Use (Blind College Student)
This intrinsic case study examined how Sara, a blind college student, used assistive technology to navigate her academic and social life. The study employed a technology biography approach, documenting Sara's history with technology from childhood through college. Key technologies included the JAWS screen reader, a Braille display, a talking tactile watch, and various software applications. The study revealed how Sara adapted technology to her needs, developed workarounds for inaccessible software, and relied on social support networks. The case was richly detailed through interviews, observation, and artifact analysis, providing a deep understanding of one person's relationship with assistive technology. The findings informed design recommendations for more accessible educational technology.

### Case 2: GPS Tracking of Parolees
This case study examined the use of GPS tracking technology with parolees in the criminal justice system. The study explored the experiences of parolees being monitored, the parole officers managing the monitoring system, and the organizational context in which the technology was deployed. The case revealed tensions between the goals of public safety and rehabilitation, the psychological impact of constant surveillance on parolees, and the challenges parole officers faced in interpreting and acting on GPS data. The study used interviews, observation, and document analysis, and demonstrated how case study research can illuminate the social and ethical dimensions of technology deployment.

### Case 3: Alzheimer's Interfaces — Laura (91-Year-Old)
This case study examined the design and use of technology interfaces for Laura, a 91-year-old woman living with Alzheimer's disease. The study explored how Laura interacted with specially designed interfaces intended to be usable by people with cognitive impairments. Through detailed observation and interviews with caregivers, the study documented the challenges of designing for users whose cognitive abilities change over time, the importance of simplicity and familiarity in interface design, and the role of caregivers in mediating technology use. The case contributed to the growing field of design for older adults and people with cognitive disabilities.

### Case 4: International Children's Digital Library (ICDL)
This embedded multiple case study examined children's use of the International Children's Digital Library across four countries over a four-year period. The unit of analysis was the individual child, with 12 children studied in depth as embedded units within the larger case of the ICDL project. The study used participant observation, interviews, and artifact analysis to understand how children from different cultural backgrounds interacted with digital books. Findings revealed cultural differences in reading preferences, navigation strategies, and social sharing behaviors. The study used literal replication (similar patterns across children within the same country) and theoretical replication (contrasting patterns across countries) to strengthen its theoretical contributions.

### Case 5: Quadriplegic Interfaces
This case study examined the design and use of computer interfaces for individuals with quadriplegia (limited or no use of their limbs). The study documented the technology strategies, workarounds, and adaptations that quadriplegic users developed to interact with computers. Through interviews and observation, the study revealed the critical importance of input device customization, the creativity of users in adapting technology to their abilities, and the gaps in mainstream software accessibility. The findings informed the development of more inclusive design guidelines.

### Case 6: Community Computing Infrastructure
This case study examined the deployment and use of community computing infrastructure — shared technology resources in community settings such as libraries, community centers, and public spaces. The study explored how community members accessed and used these resources, the social dynamics of shared technology use, and the organizational challenges of maintaining community computing infrastructure. Using interviews, observation, and document analysis, the study revealed the importance of social context, trust, and community engagement in the success of technology initiatives.

### Case 7: Massive Multiplayer Online Games (MMOGs)
This case study examined the social dynamics and technology use within massive multiplayer online games. The study explored how players formed communities, developed social norms, coordinated activities, and experienced identity and belonging in virtual worlds. Using a combination of participant observation (the researcher played the game), interviews with players, and analysis of in-game artifacts and communications, the study provided rich insights into the intersection of technology, social interaction, and play. The findings contributed to understanding of online communities, virtual economies, and collaborative technology design.

### Case 8: Fire Alert System in South Africa
This case study examined the deployment of a fire alert system in a South African community. The study explored the challenges of designing and deploying technology in resource-constrained settings, the role of community engagement in technology adoption, and the intersection of technology with local social and cultural practices. The case demonstrated how case study research can reveal context-specific factors that influence technology adoption and use, particularly in settings that differ markedly from the Western, industrialized contexts where most HCI research is conducted.

### Case 9: Music Browser Design
This case study examined the design and evaluation of a music browsing interface. The study documented the design process, user testing, and iterative refinement of the interface, providing a rich account of how design decisions were made and how user feedback was incorporated. The case demonstrated the value of case study research for understanding the design process itself, not just the outcomes of design.

### Case 10: Mobile Navigation Study
This case study examined how people used mobile navigation technology in their daily lives. The study explored the interplay between technology (GPS-enabled devices, mapping applications), physical environment (urban and rural settings), and social context (navigating alone vs. with others). Through observation, interviews, and artifact analysis, the study revealed how users integrated navigation technology with their existing spatial knowledge and social practices.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

### Error 1: Overgeneralizing from a Single Case
One of the most common criticisms of case study research is the tendency to overgeneralize findings from a single case to a broader population. Case studies achieve analytic generalization (generalizing to theory) rather than statistical generalization (generalizing to populations). A single case can confirm or challenge a theory, but the researcher should not claim that findings from one case apply to all similar cases without further evidence. Be explicit about the scope and limits of generalizability.

### Error 2: Poor Case Selection
Selecting cases based on convenience or access rather than theoretical rationale weakens the study. Cases should be selected because they are predicted to be informative for the research questions — as critical, extreme, unique, revelatory, or representative cases. In multiple case studies, cases should be selected using replication logic (literal or theoretical), not random sampling. Poor case selection can result in cases that are not actually relevant to the research questions, that are too atypical to inform theory, or that do not provide sufficient data.

### Error 3: Inadequate Triangulation
Relying on a single data source (e.g., only interviews) weakens the validity and richness of case study findings. Triangulation — using multiple sources of evidence — is a hallmark of rigorous case study research. When findings from different sources converge, confidence in the findings increases. When sources diverge, this signals areas for further investigation. Studies that rely on a single source are vulnerable to bias (e.g., social desirability in interviews, observer bias in observation) and may miss important aspects of the case.

### Error 4: Cherry-Picking Data to Support Preconceived Conclusions
Researchers may unconsciously (or consciously) select evidence that supports their preconceived conclusions while ignoring or downplaying contradictory evidence. This confirmation bias is a serious threat to the validity of case study research. Mitigate this by systematically searching for disconfirming evidence, presenting contradictory data, considering alternative explanations, and using pattern matching to compare observed and predicted patterns objectively.

### Error 5: Ignoring Rival Explanations
Failing to consider alternative explanations for the findings undermines the study's validity. For every major finding, actively seek and consider rival explanations. Ask: "What else could explain this pattern?" and look for evidence that would support or refute each rival explanation. Addressing rival explanations strengthens the study by ruling out or accounting for alternative interpretations.

### Error 6: Lack of Theoretical Basis
Case studies without a clear theoretical framework tend to be descriptive and atheoretical, producing findings that are interesting but do not contribute to knowledge building. Theoretical propositions guide data collection, focus the analysis, and provide a basis for interpreting the findings. Without them, the study risks becoming a "fishing expedition" that collects data without direction. Even exploratory case studies benefit from a conceptual framework that identifies the key concepts and relationships of interest.

### Error 7: Insufficient Protocol Development
A case study protocol outlines the procedures for data collection and analysis, ensuring consistency and rigor. Without a protocol, data collection may be inconsistent (especially across multiple cases or multiple researchers), procedures may be ad hoc, and the study's methods may be difficult to replicate. Develop a detailed protocol before beginning data collection, and use it as a guide throughout the study.

### Error 8: Treating the Case as a Sample of One
Treating a case study as a "sample of one" from which statistical generalizations can be made is a fundamental misunderstanding of case study methodology. Case studies do not represent samples from a population; they are individual cases selected for their theoretical value. The goal is analytic generalization (generalizing to theory), not statistical generalization (generalizing to populations). Confusing these two forms of generalization leads to inappropriate claims about the representativeness of the findings.

### Error 9: Neglecting Context
One of the primary advantages of case study research is its attention to context. Failing to adequately describe and analyze the context in which the case occurs undermines this advantage. The context includes the physical setting, organizational environment, social dynamics, cultural factors, historical background, and any other factors that may influence the case. Contextual information is essential for understanding the findings and for assessing their transferability to other settings.

### Error 10: Poor Writing and Presentation
A case study report should be clear, compelling, and well-structured, conveying the richness and complexity of the case while maintaining analytical rigor. Common writing problems include: presenting data without analysis (pure description), presenting analysis without sufficient supporting data (unsupported claims), failing to connect findings to the research questions and theoretical propositions, and producing reports that are too long or too detailed for the audience. The write-up should include rich, detailed descriptions and quotations that bring the case to life, while also providing clear analytical themes and conclusions.
