# Chapter 1: Introduction - Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

### Human-Computer Interaction (HCI)
HCI is a field formally founded in 1982 at the first conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems in Gaithersburg, Maryland. The field emerged when computers moved from research laboratories into homes and offices, creating a need to study how non-technical users interact with technology. HCI focuses on what people can do with computers, drawing on multiple fields that study human thinking, learning, communication, and physical object design.

### Empirical Research Contributions
Empirical contributions involve data (qualitative or quantitative) collected through various methods including experimental design, surveys, focus groups, time diaries, sensors, automated means, and ethnography. According to Wobbrock and Kientz's analysis of CHI 2016 submissions, over 70% of papers submitted were empirical studies of system use or empirical studies of people.

### Artifact Contributions
Artifact contributions involve the design and development of new artifacts including interfaces, toolkits, architectures, mock-ups, and "envisionments." These artifacts are often accompanied by empirical data about feedback or usage. This type of contribution is also known as HCI systems research, HCI interaction techniques, or HCI design prototypes. Approximately 28.4% of CHI 2016 submissions were artifact/system papers.

### Methodological Contributions
Methodological contributions are new approaches that influence processes in research or practice, including new methods, new applications of existing methods, modifications of methods, or new metrics and instruments for measurement. While there were a fair number of methodological contributions submitted to CHI 2016, submissions in other contribution categories were rare.

### Theoretical Contributions
Theoretical contributions include concepts and models that serve as vehicles for thought, which may be predictive or descriptive. These include frameworks, design spaces, and conceptual models. The Technology Acceptance Model is an example of core theory with central importance for HCI researchers focused on management information systems.

### Dataset Contributions
Dataset contributions provide a corpus for the benefit of the research community, including repositories, benchmark tasks, and actual data. Unlike other social sciences where national data sets are collected annually by government agencies, most HCI researchers must collect their own data, making HCI research particularly complex.

### Survey Contributions
Survey contributions involve review and synthesis of work done in a specific area to help identify trends and topics needing more work. This type of contribution can only occur after research in a certain area has existed for a few years, providing sufficient work to analyze.

### Opinion Contributions
Opinion contributions are writings that seek to persuade readers to change their minds, often utilizing portions of other contribution types. These writings aim not simply to inform, but to persuade.

### Micro-HCI vs. Macro-HCI
Shneiderman distinguished between micro-HCI and macro-HCI. Micro-HCI focuses on improving user experience using well-established metrics and techniques to improve task and time performance. Macro-HCI addresses broader phenomena such as motivation, collaboration, social participation, trust, and empathy, which may have societal-level impacts but are not easily measured using existing metrics or methods.

### Triangulation
Triangulation refers to the scientific practice where multiple teams examine the same research question from multiple angles over time. One data collection effort does not lead to definitive answers. When 15 teams of researchers look at similar research questions over 10 years using multiple methods and arrive at the same general conclusion, there is scientific proof for the phenomenon. The proof is strengthened when multiple research methods are used in data collection.

### Research Life Cycle
The research life cycle describes the specific steps in a research project, typically including: (1) designing research, (2) running data collection, and (3) reporting research. There is also a consideration of which methods should be utilized first when entering a new research area, with exploratory methods typically preceding more structured approaches.

### Interdisciplinary Research
HCI research is inherently interdisciplinary, drawing on expertise from computer science, sociology, psychology, communication, human factors engineering, industrial engineering, rehabilitation engineering, and many other fields. The field may be considered an interdisciplinary community, a multidisciplinary community, or its own discipline.

### QWERTY Keyboard Trade-off
The QWERTY keyboard layout exemplifies inherent trade-offs in HCI. While superior ergonomic designs exist, users have been comfortable with QWERTY for years, and alternative layouts have not been adopted despite their clear superiority from a design and usability perspective. This illustrates the tension between optimal design and user familiarity.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

### Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
The Technology Acceptance Model is a core theoretical framework with central importance for HCI researchers focused on management information systems. While well-known in MIS circles, TAM is not as familiar to HCI researchers focused on computer science. This model helps explain user acceptance of technology based on perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use.

### Human Factors Psychology Model
The human factors psychology model provides the foundation for HCI measurement, focusing on task-centered metrics including task correctness, time performance, error rate, time to learn, retention over time, and user satisfaction. These metrics are adopted by industry and standards-related organizations such as the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the International Organization for Standardization (ISO).

### User-Centered Design Framework
User-centered design emphasizes that HCI research must be practical and relevant to people, organizations, or design. The research needs to influence interface design, development processes, user training, or public policy. Due to the philosophies of the field's founders, HCI has historically focused on practical results that improve quality of life.

### Seven Types of Research Contributions Framework
Wobbrock and Kientz identified seven types of research contributions in HCI: empirical, artifact, methodological, theoretical, dataset, survey, and opinion contributions. This framework helps categorize and understand the different ways HCI research can advance the field.

### Research Life Cycle Model
The research life cycle model describes how research progresses from exploratory methods (surveys, interviews, focus groups, ethnography) to more structured methods (experimental design, automated data collection, time diaries). When entering a new research area, it is important to start with exploratory approaches before moving to more controlled methods.

### Controlled vs. Field Studies Framework
There is ongoing discussion about when controlled laboratory studies versus field studies (in-the-wild studies) are appropriate. Field studies are argued to be most appropriate for mobile device research since mobile devices are used in the field with environmental factors playing important roles. Controlled environments may not be realistic for certain technology types.

### Sociotechnical Systems Framework
This framework recognizes that phenomena cannot be reduced to two or three measurements. Multiple factors must be controlled for, and comparison studies of fundamental task recastings are much harder than comparisons of small differences in menu structure or detailed interface aspects. Design involves inherent conflicts and trade-offs rather than simple optimization.

### Stakeholder Analysis Framework
HCI involves multiple stakeholders who cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. Good HCI research helps understand the various factors at play, which design features work well for which users, and where potential conflicts or trade-offs exist among different stakeholder groups.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Identify Research Contribution Type
Before beginning HCI research, determine which type of contribution you aim to make: empirical, artifact, methodological, theoretical, dataset, survey, or opinion. Most HCI research falls into empirical research or artifact contributions, with over 70% of CHI 2016 submissions being empirical studies.

### Step 2: Select Appropriate Research Methods
Choose research methods based on your research questions. For understanding "how often?" or "how long?" questions, experimental research is often appropriate. For understanding "why?" questions, methods like surveys, time diaries, case studies, interviews, and ethnography are more suitable.

### Step 3: Consider Research Phase
When entering a new research area, start with exploratory methods before moving to structured approaches. For example, when studying how people with Down syndrome use technology, researchers first conducted exploratory surveys, then observations, then usability testing, and finally experimental design once sufficient understanding was established.

### Step 4: Determine Study Setting
Decide between controlled laboratory studies and field studies based on your research questions and technology being studied. Mobile device research may require field studies, while controlled laboratory settings may be appropriate for studying specific interface elements.

### Step 5: Identify Target Audience
Determine who your research targets: other researchers, system developers and interface designers, or public policymakers. Each audience requires different research approaches and different ways of communicating findings.

### Step 6: Recruit Appropriate Participants
Recruit participants who are representative in terms of age, educational experience, technical experience, and task domain knowledge. For studying interfaces designed for lawyers, you must have practicing lawyers participating. Recruitment is much more complex than simply finding participants and requires appropriate compensation.

### Step 7: Consider Triangulation
Plan for how your research will fit within the broader context of existing studies. One data collection effort yielding one paper is interesting but does not prove anything. Aim for your research to contribute to the triangulation of evidence from multiple teams using multiple methods over time.

### Step 8: Address Interdisciplinary Challenges
Be prepared to communicate your research methods convincingly to reviewers from different disciplinary backgrounds. When submitting to journals, conferences, or grant proposals, you never know who will review your work, and they may come from different fields with different expectations.

### Step 9: Consider Practical Implications
Ensure your research has practical relevance. HCI research must be able to influence interface design, development processes, user training, or public policy. Even if your primary target is other researchers, consider how your findings might impact practice.

### Step 10: Plan for Longitudinal Considerations
Consider whether longitudinal data collection would strengthen your research. While longitudinal studies are rare in HCI due to rapid technological change, they are valuable for understanding trends and are particularly important for policymakers who need to know if situations are improving or worsening.

## 4. Decision Criteria

### When to Use Empirical Research
Use empirical research when you need to collect data through methods like experimental design, surveys, focus groups, time diaries, sensors, automated means, or ethnography. Empirical research is appropriate when you want to understand how people interact with interfaces or when you need quantitative or qualitative data about user behavior.

### When to Use Artifact Contributions
Use artifact contributions when you want to design and develop new interfaces, toolkits, architectures, mock-ups, or envisionments. This approach is appropriate when combined with empirical data about feedback or usage, particularly when you want to demonstrate new interaction techniques or design prototypes.

### When to Use Exploratory vs. Structured Methods
Use exploratory methods (surveys, interviews, focus groups, ethnography) when entering a new research area where little is known about users and their interactions with technology. Move to structured methods (experimental design, automated data collection, time diaries) once you have established a basis of understanding from exploratory studies.

### When to Use Controlled Laboratory Studies
Use controlled laboratory studies when you need precise measurement and control over variables. These studies are appropriate for studying specific interface elements, comparing menu structures, or measuring task performance where environmental factors are not critical to the research questions.

### When to Use Field Studies
Use field studies when studying mobile or portable technology where environmental factors (weather, noise, motion, competing cognitive demands) play important roles in usage. Field studies are also appropriate when you need to understand how technologies fit into complex work, leisure, and family lives.

### When to Target Researchers
Target other researchers when your primary goal is to advance theoretical understanding or methodological approaches. This is appropriate when you want your work cited in other publications and when you're contributing to the academic discourse in HCI.

### When to Target Practitioners
Target system developers and interface designers when your research has direct implications for product design and development. This is appropriate when partnering with industry or when your findings can directly influence interaction design in corporations or nonprofit organizations.

### When to Target Policymakers
Target public policymakers when your research has implications for statutory laws, regulations, executive orders, legal cases, or human rights documents. This is appropriate for research in areas like accessibility and ergonomics where policy impacts are significant.

### When to Use Quantitative Metrics
Use quantitative metrics (task correctness, time performance, error rate, time to learn, retention over time) when the usage of computers can be broken down into specific tasks that can be measured in a quantitative and discrete way. These metrics are well-established and accepted across academic and research communities.

### When to Use Qualitative Approaches
Use qualitative approaches when studying phenomena that cannot be easily quantified, such as motivation, collaboration, social participation, trust, and empathy. These phenomena may not be measurable in laboratory settings using human factors psychology models.

### When to Use Big Data Approaches
Use big data approaches when you need to analyze larger pools of participant data (10,000–100,000 users) through sensors, text analysis, or combining datasets collected for other purposes. Big data helps determine correlations but might not help determine causality.

### When to Combine Methods
Combine big data approaches with researcher interaction with small user samples (through interviews or focus groups) to get benefits of both approaches: understanding correlations from large datasets and causality from deeper interaction with participants.

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

### Case 1: History of HCI Research
The book describes how HCI was formally founded in 1982 at the first conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems in Gaithersburg, Maryland. However, HCI-related work existed before 1982, including a 1977 project for the Social Security Administration involving task analyses, scenario generation, screen prototyping, and building a usability laboratory. Ben Shneiderman published "Software Psychology" in 1980, considered one of the first HCI books.

### Case 2: Evolution of Research Topics
The book traces how HCI research topics evolved from 1980s office automation software (word processing, database, statistical software) through graphical user interfaces in the late 1980s, to Internet and web research in the mid-1990s, to user-generated content and social networking around 2004–05, and to mobile devices, touch screens, and wearable computing in the late 2000s. By the late 2010s, research focused on collaboration, connections, emotion, and communication.

### Case 3: Changes in Research Methods
The book explains how research methods evolved as tools became less expensive (eye-tracking, sensors, drones, facial EMG, EEG) and new tools developed (Amazon's Mechanical Turk). New opportunities emerged with social networking providing billions of text and multimedia pieces for analysis. Some research became fully automated, such as citation analysis using Google Scholar.

### Case 4: Micro-HCI vs. Macro-HCI Examples
Shneiderman's distinction is illustrated with examples: Micro-HCI involves improving user experience with established metrics for task and time performance. Macro-HCI addresses broader phenomena like Wikipedia's 70,000 active contributors who spend their own time creating and editing entries—what causes them to do so? Task and time performance would not be appropriate metrics for understanding this behavior.

### Case 5: Interdisciplinary Challenges Example
The book describes how different disciplines focus on different aspects of research: sociology-based HCI research focuses on demographic representativeness and random sampling; psychology-based HCI research focuses on ideal and clean research design; computer science-based HCI research focuses on interface implications and technical underpinnings; design-based HCI research focuses on look and feel.

### Case 6: Target Audience Examples
The book provides examples of different target audiences: (1) Researchers focus on citation metrics and impact factors; (2) Industrial practitioners focus on evaluating their own products with specific configurations; (3) Policymakers need data about how many people are impacted and which specific laws or policies relate to HCI work.

### Case 7: Research Life Cycle Example
The book describes a research project studying how people with Down syndrome use technology: first conducted exploratory surveys to understand technology use, then observations of expert users, then usability testing of social networking and touch screens, and finally experimental design to understand authentication methods. Starting with experimental design would have been premature given the lack of existing knowledge.

### Case 8: Controlled vs. Field Studies Example
The book describes three research studies of a web-based security prototype in controlled settings (university lab, workplace, home) with consistent laptops showing over 90% average task performance. When the same prototype was placed on the web with diverse users, lower technical experience, and varied technical environments, average task performance dropped to under 50%.

### Case 9: Menu Design Research
The "Depth vs Breadth in Menus" sidebar describes how multiple research studies consistently found broad, shallow tree structures superior to narrow, deep structures. Reasons include users getting more frustrated navigating more levels, users being capable of dealing with more than 7±2 options (since menus deal with recognition, not recall), and scanning strategies leading to superior performance.

### Case 10: Usability Testing Debate
The book mentions the ongoing debate about minimum number of people required for usability testing. The commonly repeated number of 5 users is not actually supported by research. More recent studies suggest 10±2 users or even more than 10 users, indicating this remains an unresolved research question.

### Case 11: QWERTY Keyboard Trade-off
The QWERTY keyboard exemplifies inherent HCI trade-offs. Superior ergonomic designs exist with key layouts allowing faster typing, but the QWERTY layout persists because users have been comfortable with it for years. This illustrates the tension between optimal design and user familiarity, and the short-term performance decrease when introducing totally new interfaces.

### Case 12: Usability vs. Security Trade-off
The book describes the intersection of usability and security as an inherent conflict. HCI wants 100% easy-to-use interfaces while security professionals want 100% secure systems. Many security features are designed to present roadblocks and be hard. The best way to make a 100% usable interface would be to remove all security features, which is clearly impossible.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

### Error 1: Assuming One Study Proves Scientific Truth
One data collection effort yielding one paper does not prove anything. Scientific truth requires multiple teams examining the same research question from multiple angles over time. Researchers should never get disappointed when finding another team working on similar questions—this strengthens findings through triangulation.

### Error 2: Using Inappropriate Participants
A common error is recruiting college students for research that requires domain-specific participants. While appropriate for studying motor performance or college students themselves, much HCI research requires participants representative in terms of age, educational experience, technical experience, and task domain knowledge. Studying lawyer interfaces requires practicing lawyers.

### Error 3: Applying Quantitative Metrics to Qualitative Phenomena
Classic metrics like task correctness and time performance measure "how often?" or "how long?" but not "why?" These metrics are inappropriate for phenomena like Wikipedia contributors' motivations, enjoyment, emotional gain, or feelings of community. Researchers must recognize when phenomena require qualitative approaches.

### Error 4: Starting with Structured Methods in New Research Areas
Beginning with experimental design when little is known about a research area is premature. Exploratory methods (surveys, interviews, focus groups, ethnography) should establish understanding before moving to structured methods. The controls necessary for experimental design require understanding unknown phenomena first.

### Error 5: Ignoring Interdisciplinary Perspectives
Researchers from different disciplines have different expectations and value systems. Computer science focuses on practical outcomes; management information systems focuses on theory; sociology focuses on demographic representativeness. Failing to consider these different perspectives can lead to rejection when work is reviewed by people from different backgrounds.

### Error 6: Misidentifying Target Audience
Most researchers unknowingly target other researchers, but HCI research can also target practitioners or policymakers. Each audience requires different research approaches and communication strategies. Research targeted at policymakers needs specific coverage of affected populations and relevant laws, not generalizations.

### Error 7: Overlooking Longitudinal Value
Longitudinal studies are rare in HCI due to rapid technological change, but this is a real shortcoming that limits the value communities outside computer science place on HCI research. Fields like medicine track health outcomes over decades, and national census data collection occurs over centuries, but such longitudinal data generally doesn't exist in HCI.

### Error 8: Conflating Correlation with Causality
Big data can help determine correlations (where there are relationships) but might not help determine causality (why there are relationships). With 100,000 users, researchers may never interact with participants or get deeper understanding of data meaning. Combining big data with small-sample interaction (interviews, focus groups) provides benefits of both approaches.

### Error 9: Assuming Laboratory Results Transfer to Real World
Controlled laboratory studies may show over 90% task performance, but the same system in real-world conditions with diverse users and varied technical environments may show under 50% performance. Researchers must recognize that laboratory conditions may not reflect real-world usage contexts.

### Error 10: Neglecting Stakeholder Conflicts
HCI involves multiple stakeholders who cannot all be satisfied simultaneously. Good HCI research must understand where potential conflicts or trade-offs exist. For example, usability wants easy interfaces while security wants hard-to-bypass features. Sustainability conflicts with the drive for new and better devices.

### Error 11: Ignoring Disciplinary Publication Norms
Different disciplines have different publication preferences: computer science focuses on conference publications while management information systems focuses on journal publications. Some disciplines focus on single-author publications while others focus on group-author publications. Not understanding these norms can disadvantage researchers when their work is evaluated.

### Error 12: Over-Reliance on Traditional Methods
The old methods of hypothesis testing, statistical tests, and control groups come from a proud history of scientific research but are not sufficient to measure all of today's phenomena. The same applies to traditional metrics of task correctness and time performance. Researchers must be open to new methods from social sciences and other fields.

### Error 13: Misunderstanding Big Data Limitations
While big data approaches allow analysis of 10,000–100,000 users, participants may never interact with researchers, be aware their data is included, or get to clarify data meaning. Researchers have no opportunity to interact with participants and may find it hard to get deeper understanding of data meaning from participants themselves.

### Error 14: Underestimating Recruitment Complexity
Participant recruitment is much more complex than "find some people." It requires identifying representative participants, compensating them appropriately, and managing the logistics of involving domain experts. For studying lawyer interfaces, you may need actual practicing lawyers with right task domain knowledge, which takes time and appropriate payment.

### Error 15: Failing to Consider Ecological Impact
HCI researchers rarely consider the ecological impact of their research or new interfaces. Changing all keyboards to DVORAK layout might improve performance but would result in millions of keyboards in landfills. Sustainability means encouraging users to limit energy usage, keep using current devices, and reduce technology waste.
