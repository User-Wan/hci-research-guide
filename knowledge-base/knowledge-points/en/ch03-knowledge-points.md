# Chapter 3: Experimental Research - Knowledge Points

## 1. Core Concept Definitions

### True Experiment
A true experiment involves multiple groups or conditions where participants are randomly assigned to each condition. True experiments possess characteristics including: based on at least one testable research hypothesis, have at least two conditions (treatment and control), dependent variables measured through quantitative measurements, results analyzed through statistical significance tests, designed to remove potential biases, and replicable with different participants, times, locations, and experimenters.

### Quasi-Experiment
A quasi-experiment involves multiple groups or conditions but participants are not randomly assigned to different conditions. This design lacks the randomization component that characterizes true experiments, which limits the ability to establish causal relationships.

### Nonexperiment
A nonexperiment involves only one observation group or one condition. This design cannot establish causal relationships or compare different treatments because there is no comparison group.

### Between-Group Design
Between-group design (also called "between-subject design") requires each participant to be exposed to only one experimental condition. The number of participant groups directly corresponds to the number of experimental conditions. This design is cleaner, avoids learning effects, and is less likely to be affected by fatigue and frustration, but requires larger sample sizes due to individual differences.

### Within-Group Design
Within-group design (also called "within-subject design") requires each participant to be exposed to multiple experimental conditions. Only one group of participants is needed for the entire experiment. This design effectively isolates individual differences and requires fewer participants, but is more vulnerable to learning effects and fatigue.

### Split-Plot Design
Split-plot design has both between-group components and within-group components. One or more independent variables are investigated through a between-group approach while other variables are investigated through a within-group approach. This hybrid design combines advantages of both approaches.

### Factorial Design
Factorial design is used when an experiment investigates more than one independent variable. The number of conditions is determined by multiplying the number of levels of each independent variable. This design allows simultaneous investigation of all independent variables and interaction effects between them.

### Learning Effect
Learning effect occurs when participants improve their performance due to practice or experience gained during the experiment. In within-group designs, participants who complete tasks under one condition may perform better under subsequent conditions due to learning, potentially biasing results.

### Fatigue Effect
Fatigue effect occurs when participants' performance deteriorates due to tiredness or boredom during lengthy experiments. In within-group designs, conditions tested later may show worse performance than earlier conditions due to fatigue.

### Latin Square Design
Latin Square Design is used to counterbalance condition orders in within-group experiments. Each condition appears only once in each row and column of the design matrix, ensuring order effects are balanced across participants.

### Interaction Effect
Interaction effect occurs when the effect of one independent variable on the dependent variable depends on the particular level of another independent variable. When significant interaction exists, the means of the dependent variable are determined jointly by multiple independent variables.

### Random Errors
Random errors (also called "chance errors" or "noise") occur by chance and are not correlated with actual values. They push observed values up or down around the exact value in both directions. Random errors can be reduced by enlarging sample size.

### Systematic Errors (Biases)
Systematic errors (biases) always push observed values in the same direction, unlike random errors which fluctuate in both directions. Biases never offset each other and cause observed means to be consistently too high or too low. They are the true enemy of experimental research.

### Confounding Variables
Confounding variables are factors other than independent variables that may impact dependent variables. In true experiments, these should be kept constant across conditions. When studying age differences, confounding factors include educational background, computer experience, frequency of use, and living conditions.

### Wizard-of-Oz Approach
The Wizard-of-Oz approach uses a human to simulate system behavior when testing ideal applications that don't exist in reality. For example, a human acts as a speech recognizer, entering what users say into the system. Participants believe they're interacting with the actual system until debriefing.

### Pilot Study
A pilot study is a preliminary experiment conducted to test design, systems, and instruments before actual data collection. It should be treated seriously and conducted exactly as planned for the actual experiment, with participants from the target population. Pilot studies are critical for identifying potential biases.

## 2. Theoretical Frameworks

### Three Types of Studies Framework
Studies can be categorized into three types: (1) True experiments with multiple conditions and random assignment; (2) Quasi-experiments with multiple conditions but no random assignment; (3) Nonexperiments with only one observation group or condition. This framework helps researchers understand the level of control and causal inference possible with different designs.

### Between-Group vs. Within-Group Design Framework
This framework presents the trade-offs between design approaches. Between-group design is cleaner and avoids learning effects but requires larger samples due to individual differences. Within-group design isolates individual differences and requires fewer participants but is vulnerable to learning and fatigue effects. The choice depends on task nature, participant pool, and research questions.

### Factorial Design Framework
Factorial design allows investigation of multiple independent variables simultaneously. The number of conditions equals the product of levels of each independent variable (C = V₁ × V₂ × ... × Vₙ). This framework enables study of main effects and interaction effects between variables.

### Split-Plot Design Framework
Split-plot design combines between-group and within-group components. One variable uses between-group approach (different participants for different levels) while another uses within-group approach (same participants experience all levels). This framework is useful when some variables naturally require between-group treatment (like age) while others can be within-group (like interface conditions).

### Error Types Framework
Experimental errors are categorized into random errors (chance variations that offset with larger samples) and systematic errors (biases that consistently push results in one direction). This framework helps researchers understand that while random errors are unavoidable, systematic errors must be identified and controlled.

### Bias Sources Framework
Systematic errors come from five major sources: measurement instruments (inaccurate or misconfigured), experimental procedures (unclear instructions, non-randomized order), participants (non-representative samples, stress), experimenter behavior (spoken language, body language, inconsistency), and environmental factors (physical and social). This framework guides bias identification and control.

### Interaction Effects Framework
Interaction effects occur when the effect of one variable depends on the level of another variable. This framework emphasizes that performance may be affected by multiple factors jointly, and significant interactions can have important design implications (e.g., touchscreen better for brief interactions, mouse better for long-term use).

### Experimental Lifecycle Framework
The experimental lifecycle includes: identify research hypothesis, specify study design, run pilot study, recruit participants, run data collection sessions, analyze data, and report results. Within each session: greet participants, introduce study, get consent, assign conditions, training tasks, actual tasks, questionnaires, debriefing, and payment.

## 3. Methodology Steps

### Step 1: Identify Research Hypothesis
Start with a clearly defined research hypothesis with reasonable scope. The hypothesis should specify independent and dependent variables. Use exploratory studies (observations, interviews, focus groups) to identify key research issues and appropriate variables before designing the experiment.

### Step 2: Determine Number of Independent Variables
Answer how many independent variables to investigate and how many levels each has. This determines whether you need basic design (one variable) or factorial design (multiple variables), and the total number of conditions (product of all variable levels).

### Step 3: Select Design Approach
Choose between between-group, within-group, or split-plot design based on: task nature (simple vs. complex), individual differences (small vs. large), learning effect susceptibility, fatigue potential, and participant pool size. Consider hybrid designs when appropriate.

### Step 4: Control Independent Variables
Determine how to manipulate independent variables to create experimental conditions. For some variables (like device types), control is straightforward. For others (like speech recognition errors), consider Wizard-of-Oz approaches or other methods to achieve desired conditions.

### Step 5: Implement Counterbalancing
Use Latin Square Design to counterbalance condition orders in within-group experiments. This ensures learning effects and fatigue are balanced across conditions. Each condition appears only once in each row and column of the design matrix.

### Step 6: Address Learning Effects
For within-group designs vulnerable to learning effects: provide sufficient training time before actual tasks (learning curve is steeper initially), use Latin Square Design to counterbalance orders, and consider whether tasks are susceptible to learning (complex learned skills are less susceptible).

### Step 7: Manage Fatigue
Design experiments frugally to reduce task numbers and shorten duration. Keep sessions to 60-90 minutes maximum (definitely under 2 hours). Provide breaks as needed, and consider forcing breaks since participants may not realize they're tired.

### Step 8: Control Systematic Errors
Address bias from five sources: use accurate measurement instruments (prefer software-driven), prepare detailed written procedures and instructions, recruit representative participants carefully, train experimenters to be neutral and professional, and control physical and social environment.

### Step 9: Conduct Pilot Studies
Run pilot studies with target population participants before actual data collection. Treat pilot studies seriously—having team members complete tasks is not a true pilot study. Use pilots to identify and fix overlooked biases and problems.

### Step 10: Run Experiment Sessions
Follow the experimental lifecycle: ensure systems are functioning, greet participants, introduce study purpose and procedures, get consent, assign conditions per randomization method, conduct training tasks, actual tasks, questionnaires, debriefing, and payment.

### Step 11: Analyze Data
Use appropriate statistical methods for your design. Between-group designs require different analyses than within-group designs. For factorial designs, analyze main effects and interaction effects. Report results with appropriate statistics (F-values, t-values, p-values).

### Step 12: Report Results
Report findings carefully, acknowledging limitations and potential biases even when results are statistically significant. Describe the design, participants, procedures, and analysis methods in detail to enable replication.

## 4. Decision Criteria

### When to Use Between-Group Design
Use between-group design when: tasks are simple with limited individual differences; tasks would be greatly influenced by learning effect; or problems cannot be investigated through within-group design (e.g., comparing novice vs. experienced users, or users from different cultures).

### When to Use Within-Group Design
Use within-group design when: tasks have large individual differences (complex cognitive tasks); tasks are less susceptible to learning effect (typing, reading, problem solving); or target participant pool is very small (people with specific disabilities, busy professionals).

### When to Use Split-Plot Design
Use split-plot design when you have multiple independent variables where some naturally require between-group treatment (like age groups) while others can be within-group (like interface conditions). This combines advantages of both approaches.

### When to Use Factorial Design
Use factorial design when investigating two or more independent variables simultaneously. This allows study of main effects and interaction effects, which may reveal important findings not apparent when studying variables individually.

### When to Conduct Pilot Studies
Always conduct pilot studies before actual data collection. No matter how well you plan, there are always overlooked issues. Pilot studies are the only chance to fix mistakes before running the main study. They should be conducted exactly as planned for the actual experiment.

### When to Use Wizard-of-Oz Approach
Use Wizard-of-Oz when testing ideal applications that don't exist in reality (like error-free speech recognition). A human simulates system behavior while participants believe they're interacting with the actual system. Be aware that humans also make errors, making it difficult to achieve perfect control.

### When to Force Breaks
Force breaks during experiments even when participants don't request them. Participants may not realize they're getting tired and tend to ignore suggestions to take breaks. Forced breaks help reduce fatigue effects, especially in lengthy within-group designs.

### When to Use Written Instructions
Use written documents or prerecorded instructions for both participants and experimenters. This ensures consistency across sessions, especially when multiple experimenters are involved. Slightly different wording in instructions can lead to different participant responses.

### When to Randomize Condition Order
Always randomize condition order in within-group and split-plot designs. Non-randomized order introduces systematic bias from learning effects (favoring later conditions) or fatigue (favoring earlier conditions). Latin Square Design is the standard approach.

### When to Recruit Representative Participants
Recruit participants carefully to ensure the pool is representative of the target user population. Participants from technical websites may outperform general public. Prescreening helps make groups as similar as possible except for experimental variables.

### When to Reschedule Sessions
Reschedule sessions if participants arrive tired, exhausted, or very nervous. Give them time to recover or schedule for another time. Participant state can introduce systematic errors that bias results.

### When to Have Two Experimenters
Have two experimenters supervise sessions when possible: one as lead (interacting with participants) and one as assistant (observing, fixing errors, taking notes). This improves quality control and provides backup if issues arise.

### When Between-Group Design is the Only Option
Between-group design is the only option when participants cannot experience all conditions: comparing novice vs. experienced users (one person cannot be both), users from different cultures (one person represents only one culture), or pre-existing conditions (people with vs. without disabilities).

## 5. Book Cases and Examples

### Case 1: Pointing Device and Icon Size Experiment
The book presents a hypothesis about target selection speed using mouse, joystick, or trackball to select icons of different sizes (small, medium, large). This results in two independent variables with three levels each, creating 3 × 3 = 9 conditions. This illustrates how the number of conditions is determined by the product of variable levels.

### Case 2: QWERTY vs. DVORAK vs. Alphabetic Keyboards
The book uses a keyboard comparison experiment to illustrate single independent variable design. Three conditions (QWERTY, DVORAK, Alphabetic) can be studied with between-group design (three groups, each using one keyboard) or within-group design (one group using all three keyboards).

### Case 3: ATM Button vs. Touch-Screen Interface
The book describes a within-group experiment evaluating two ATM types (button vs. touch-screen) for money withdrawal tasks. If the participant first uses the button interface, they gain experience that may improve performance on the touch-screen interface, illustrating the learning effect problem.

### Case 4: Age and GPS Assistance Study
The book presents a split-plot design studying age (20-40, 41-60, above 60) and GPS use (with vs. without). Age is between-group (different participants for each age range) while GPS use is within-group (each participant drives both with and without GPS). This illustrates the hybrid design approach.

### Case 5: Window Switching Methods Study
The book describes a 3 ×  factorial design by Warr et al. (2016) investigating window switching methods (Cards, Exposé, Mosaic interfaces) and number of open windows (3, 6, 9). The between-group factor was switching method; within-group factor was window count. Learning and fatigue were controlled through practice and Latin Square Design.

### Case 6: Mouse vs. Touchscreen Interaction Effect
The book illustrates interaction effects with device type (mouse, touchscreen) and user experience (novice, experienced). Novice users are faster with touchscreen; experienced users are faster with mouse. Speed increases more for mouse than touchscreen with experience. This interaction suggests touchscreen for brief interactions (ATM), mouse for long-term use (desktop).

### Case 7: Typing Speed Measurement Example
The book uses typing speed measurement to illustrate random vs. systematic errors. Observed values (46, 52, 47, 51, 53 wpm) fluctuate around actual value (50 wpm) due to random errors. Under biased conditions (tiredness), values (47, 44, 45, 42, 46 wpm) consistently fall below actual value due to systematic error.

### Case 8: Speech Recognition Wizard-of-Oz
The book describes using Wizard-of-Oz to test ideal speech recognition (error-free condition). A human listens to user dictation and enters it into the system. Participants believe they're interacting with a speech recognizer. This approach has limitations since humans also make errors in listening and typing.

### Case 9: PDA Data Entry Positioning
The book mentions an experiment studying data entry on PDA where physical positioning wasn't specified. Some participants held PDA in one hand, others placed it on table. Differences in screen distance, angle, and hands involved could bias results, illustrating the need for detailed procedures.

### Case 10: Experimenter Bias Scenarios
The book presents four scenarios of experimenter bias: (1) Saying "I think you will really like them... I designed them myself!" may discourage negative feedback; (2) Saying "It's slower than a snail" reveals negative attitude; (3) Tapping fingers and staring impatiently shows frustration; (4) Arriving late and being unprepared gives negative impression. All can bias participant responses.

### Case 11: CNET Website Participant Recruitment
The book describes recruiting participants by posting on CNET website for a mobile phone interface study. Since CNET features technical news, visitors have strong technical backgrounds, potentially causing observed data to outperform general public. This illustrates the importance of representative participant recruitment.

### Case 12: Time Stress Instruction Wording
The book reports an HCI study where participants instructed to complete tasks "as quickly as possible" under one condition and "take your time, there is no rush" under another. Interestingly, the no-time-stress condition resulted in faster completion, demonstrating how instruction wording can significantly impact results.

## 6. Common Errors and Pitfalls

### Error 1: Confusing Quasi-Experiments with True Experiments
Not randomly assigning participants to conditions makes a study a quasi-experiment, not a true experiment. This limits the ability to establish causal relationships. Researchers should clearly identify their study type and acknowledge limitations accordingly.

### Error 2: Using Within-Group Design for Learning-Susceptible Tasks
When tasks are highly susceptible to learning effects (like learning website navigation architecture), within-group design is inappropriate. Participants who complete tasks under one condition gain knowledge that affects performance under other conditions, biasing results toward later conditions.

### Error 3: Ignoring Individual Differences in Between-Group Designs
Between-group designs are weaker due to high noise from individual differences. The difference between conditions can be buried in this noise, making it harder to detect significant effects and increasing Type II error risk. Larger sample sizes are needed to overcome this limitation.

### Error 4: Not Counterbalancing Condition Orders
Failing to use Latin Square Design in within-group experiments introduces systematic bias from learning effects (favoring later conditions) or fatigue (favoring earlier conditions). Counterbalancing ensures order effects are balanced across conditions.

### Error 5: Insufficient Training Before Actual Tasks
Not providing adequate training before actual tasks in within-group designs increases learning effects during the experiment. Research shows learning curves are steeper initially, so sufficient training time before data collection reduces learning effects during actual tasks.

### Error 6: Experiment Sessions Too Long
Sessions lasting over 90 minutes (and definitely over 2 hours) increase fatigue effects. Participants may get tired or frustrated, deteriorating performance in later conditions. Design experiments frugally to reduce task numbers and shorten duration.

### Error 7: Not Forcing Breaks
Even when experimenters encourage breaks, participants may not realize they're tired and ignore suggestions. Forced breaks help reduce fatigue effects, especially in lengthy within-group designs where fatigue can significantly bias results.

### Error 8: Using Inaccurate Measurement Instruments
Inaccurate or misconfigured measurement instruments introduce systematic errors. For example, a slow stopwatch consistently records less time than actual, making observed performance appear better than reality. Use software-driven instruments to avoid human errors and ensure accuracy.

### Error 9: Inconsistent Instructions Across Sessions
When multiple experimenters give slightly different instructions, participants may respond differently. One experimenter's longer training or different wording can systematically affect results. Use written documents or prerecorded instructions to ensure consistency.

### Error 10: Non-Representative Participant Samples
Recruiting from specialized populations (like technical websites) may not represent target users. Participants with strong technical backgrounds may outperform general public. Carefully recruit to ensure participant pool is representative of target user population.

### Error 11: Experimenter Bias Through Verbal and Nonverbal Cues
Experimenters may unknowingly influence participants through spoken language ("I designed them myself"), body language (tapping fingers impatiently), or facial expressions. These cues can bias participant responses, especially subjective ratings. Train experimenters to be neutral, calm, and patient.

### Error 12: Unprofessional Experimenter Behavior
Arriving late, being unprepared, or appearing disorganized gives participants negative impressions that may affect their performance and subjective ratings. Experimenters should arrive early, have everything ready, and maintain professional demeanor throughout sessions.

### Error 13: Not Controlling Environmental Factors
Physical factors (noise, temperature, lighting) and social factors (people watching over shoulder) can introduce systematic errors. Loud noise may distract participants or affect speech recognition. Ensure lab is quiet, well-lit, comfortable, and participants are seated alone when possible.

### Error 14: Skipping Pilot Studies
Pilot studies are critical for identifying overlooked biases and problems. Having team members complete tasks is not a true pilot study. Conduct pilots with target population participants, treating them seriously and following the same procedures planned for actual experiments.

### Error 15: Not Acknowledging Limitations in Reports
Even well-designed experiments have biases that cannot be completely eliminated. Researchers should acknowledge limitations and potential biases when reporting results, even when findings are statistically significant. This maintains scientific integrity and helps others interpret findings appropriately.
