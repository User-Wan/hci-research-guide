# Chapter 7 Summary: Diary Studies (Case Studies)

## Main Topic

This chapter provides a comprehensive treatment of case study research methodology in the context of human-computer interaction (HCI). It explains how case studies — in-depth investigations of a small number of cases within their real-life context — provide rich, detailed understanding of how technology is used, might be used, or impacts users. The chapter covers the rationale for case study research, different types of case studies, research design, data collection, analysis, and reporting.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Case Study Definition**: A case study is an in-depth, in-context examination of a small number of cases, using multiple data sources analyzed through qualitative methods to build nuanced descriptions capturing the complexities of the environments in question. Four key aspects characterize case studies: in-depth investigation of a small number of cases; examination in context; multiple data sources; and emphasis on qualitative data and analysis.

- **Intrinsic vs. Instrumental Case Studies**: Intrinsic case studies describe cases that are of interest to a particular situation (e.g., studying a specific client's work processes). Instrumental case studies ask questions hoping to generate insights that go beyond the case at hand, using the case as a tool for broader understanding. A single study can be both intrinsic and instrumental.

- **Embedded vs. Holistic Case Studies**: An embedded case study includes multiple units of analysis within a single case (e.g., studying multiple tasks performed by one person). A holistic case study addresses only one unit per case. The choice depends on the nature of the research questions being asked.

- **Literal vs. Theoretical Replication**: In multiple-case studies, literal replication uses closely comparable cases to demonstrate consistency of results. Theoretical replication uses cases with specific differences to generate results that differ in explainable ways, similar to controlled experiments.

- **Ecological Validity**: Case studies focus on observation of phenomena in meaningful contexts beyond the investigator's control, providing realistic pictures of how people actually work with technology, as opposed to the contrived environments of usability labs.

## Main Methods and Frameworks

**Goals of Case Studies** (four categories):
- **Exploration**: Understanding novel problems or situations, often informing new designs. Used when a project team has incomplete understanding of a problem and its context.
- **Explanation**: Developing models to understand contexts of technology use. Examines how technologies are used in ways not anticipated in the original design.
- **Description**: Documenting a system, context of technology use, or the design process. Includes describing design methodologies and lessons learned.
- **Demonstration**: Showing how a new tool was successfully used. Often shorter and less in-depth than descriptive studies, complementing other evaluation methods.

**Data Collection Sources**:
- Documentation and archival records (for understanding past and current practices)
- Interviews (for understanding perceptions, concerns, needs)
- Direct observation (for understanding what people do)
- Participant observation (for understanding complex dynamics)
- Artifacts (physical and digital objects bridging computer work and daily life)
- Technology biographies combining demonstrations, personal histories, and guided speculation

**Case Selection Strategies**:
- Representative cases (for instrumental studies aiming at generalization)
- Extreme or edge cases (for gaining fresh perspectives on established problems)
- Critical cases (that are particularly distinctive with respect to the problem)
- Convenience cases (for exploratory studies where any case might work)
- Screening surveys to identify promising candidates from large pools

**Research Design Components**:
- Research questions describing study goals
- Hypotheses or propositions stating expected findings
- Units of analysis defining study granularity
- Data analysis plan (developed before data collection begins)

**Analysis Techniques**:
- Bottom-up analysis using grounded theory to code and categorize data
- Pattern matching between observations and theoretical predictions
- Matrix displays for organizing and comparing multiple units of analysis
- Data triangulation across multiple sources to increase confidence in interpretations
- Consideration of rival explanations to strengthen conclusions

**Case Study Protocol**:
- Introduction with questions and hypotheses
- Data collection procedures and criteria for choosing cases
- Contact information and logistical plans
- Specific questions and methods for each data source
- Outline of the final report

## Important Takeaways

1. **Case studies succeed through deep, narrow investigation**: Unlike controlled experiments that find general answers to narrow questions, case studies provide thorough exploration of a small set of cases, revealing design particulars that might go unnoticed in usability labs.

2. **Multiple data sources enable triangulation**: Using interviews, observations, artifacts, and archival records together provides corroborating evidence that increases confidence in findings. When sources converge, interpretations are strengthened; when they diverge or contradict, deeper investigation is needed.

3. **Generalization requires caution**: Case study results may not generalize to broader populations. Even with multiple cases providing consistent findings, researchers should avoid broad claims and instead suggest that trends merit further investigation with more rigorous sampling.

4. **Informal case studies serve practical purposes**: When formal studies are overkill, informal case studies with carefully chosen participants can provide valuable feedback for understanding new situations, testing initial designs, or validating completed work. They trade scientific rigor for ease of data collection.

5. **Theoretical grounding strengthens case studies**: Even exploratory studies benefit from research questions, hypotheses, and propositions that guide data collection and analysis. Ignoring theory in favor of indiscriminate data collection is a recipe for failure.

6. **Storytelling is central to case study reporting**: Carefully chosen anecdotes, direct quotes, and vignettes bring concrete details to discussions, supporting analytic results. The case study's power lies in using details of specific situations to tell stories behind HCI research questions.

7. **Extreme cases offer unique insights**: Studying atypical or marginalized users (such as parolees tracked by GPS) can reveal perspectives and concerns that investigation of expected cases would miss, enriching understanding of technology's social and cultural impacts.
