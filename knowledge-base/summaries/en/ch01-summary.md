# Chapter 1: Introduction — Summary

## Main Topic

This chapter provides a comprehensive overview of the field of Human-Computer Interaction (HCI) research. It traces the historical evolution of HCI from its formal founding in the early 1980s, explains the interdisciplinary nature of the field, categorizes the types of research contributions that are valued, and examines how both the topics and methods of HCI research have changed over time. The chapter also addresses who the audiences for HCI research are, the importance of triangulation and the research lifecycle, and the inherent trade-offs that HCI researchers must navigate.

## Key Concepts

**History and Origins of HCI:** The field of HCI was formally founded in 1982 at the first conference on Human Factors in Computing Systems. However, its roots extend further back, drawing on work in office automation, human factors, psychology, and computer science. The shift from mainframes used by trained specialists to personal computers used by the general public created an urgent need for a discipline focused on ease of use.

**Seven Types of Research Contributions:** Wobbrock and Kientz identified seven types of HCI research contributions: empirical contributions (data collected through experiments, surveys, etc.), artifact contributions (design and development of new interfaces or toolkits), methodological contributions (new or modified research methods), theoretical contributions (frameworks and conceptual models), dataset contributions (corpora for the research community), survey contributions (reviews and syntheses of existing work), and opinion contributions (writings aimed at persuasion). The majority of HCI research falls into either empirical or artifact categories.

**Micro-HCI vs. Macro-HCI:** Shneiderman's distinction between micro-HCI and macro-HCI is a central concept. Micro-HCI focuses on task-centered metrics such as task correctness, time performance, and error rate. Macro-HCI addresses broader phenomena like motivation, collaboration, trust, and societal-level impacts, which are harder to measure using traditional laboratory methods and require multimethod approaches.

**Interdisciplinary Nature:** HCI draws on computer science, psychology, sociology, communication, human factors engineering, and many other disciplines. Each discipline brings different expectations, value systems, and research preferences. Understanding these differences is essential for effective interdisciplinary collaboration and for communicating research to diverse reviewers.

**Triangulation and the Research Lifecycle:** No single research study provides definitive answers. Scientific truth in HCI is established through triangulation — multiple research teams examining the same questions from different angles, using different methods, over time. The research lifecycle describes how exploratory methods (surveys, interviews, ethnography) should often precede more structured methods (experimental design, automated data collection).

## Main Methods and Frameworks Discussed

The chapter introduces a broad taxonomy of research methods used in HCI, including experimental design, surveys, focus groups, time diaries, sensors, ethnography, and automated data collection. It discusses the evolution from small-scale laboratory studies to big data approaches involving tens of thousands of users. The chapter also outlines the research lifecycle framework, which guides researchers on the appropriate sequencing of methods — starting with exploratory studies and progressing to more controlled investigations. The concept of field studies versus controlled laboratory studies is introduced, with the recommendation that both approaches be combined for richer understanding.

## Important Takeaways

**HCI research must be both rigorous and relevant.** Unlike some fields that prioritize theoretical results, HCI research should influence interface design, development processes, user training, or public policy. The practical impact of research is a core value of the field.

**Audience awareness matters.** HCI researchers typically target other researchers, but research can also be directed toward practitioners (systems developers and designers) or public policymakers. Each audience requires different research approaches and communication strategies. Policymakers, for example, value longitudinal data, specific coverage statistics, and face-to-face engagement.

**Trade-offs are inherent in HCI design.** There are fundamental tensions between usability and security, between innovation and user consistency, and between sustainability and technological progress. The QWERTY keyboard example illustrates how user familiarity can override superior design. These trade-offs mean that HCI design is not a simple optimization problem but requires balancing multiple stakeholder needs.

**Big data and small studies serve complementary purposes.** Big data helps identify correlations across large populations but may not reveal causality. Smaller studies with direct participant interaction provide deeper understanding of meaning and motivation. Combining both approaches yields the most complete picture.

**Longitudinal research is a significant gap in HCI.** Unlike fields such as medicine or sociology, HCI lacks extensive longitudinal datasets. This limits the ability to track trends over time and reduces the credibility of HCI research in some academic communities. Addressing this gap is an important opportunity for the field.

**One study does not make a fact.** A single research project at a single point in time is interesting but proves nothing. It is the accumulation of evidence through replication, diverse methods, and multiple research teams that establishes scientific truth in HCI.
