# Chapter 5 Summary: Usability Testing (Surveys)

## Main Topic

This chapter provides a comprehensive treatment of survey research methodology in the context of human-computer interaction (HCI). It covers the full lifecycle of survey research — from defining research goals and target populations, through sampling strategies and questionnaire design, to pilot testing and response rate optimization. The chapter emphasizes that while surveys are among the most commonly used research methods, their validity depends critically on proper methodology at every stage.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Survey vs. Questionnaire**: A distinction is drawn between the "questionnaire" (the set of questions) and the "survey" (the complete methodological approach encompassing sampling, reminders, incentives, and implementation). Though often used interchangeably, understanding this distinction helps researchers attend to all aspects of survey design.

- **Target Population and Sampling Frame**: The "target population" refers to the specific group of individuals the survey aims to study, defined by inclusion and exclusion criteria. The "sampling frame" is the actual list or directory used to identify and contact potential respondents (e.g., membership directories, email lists, phone books).

- **Probabilistic vs. Nonprobabilistic Sampling**: A fundamental dichotomy in survey methodology. Probabilistic sampling (including random sampling and stratified sampling) enables population estimates with known confidence levels and margins of error. Nonprobabilistic sampling (convenience, snowball, self-selected) does not support population estimates but is widely accepted in HCI research where strict random sampling is often impractical.

- **Response Rate and Validity**: The chapter stresses that sufficient response rates are essential for data validity, and outlines multiple strategies (precontact letters, multiple reminders, incentives, ease of return) to improve them.

## Main Methods and Frameworks

**Sampling Approaches**:
- **Census**: Attempting to survey every member of a population — expensive and rare, typically sponsored by governments.
- **Random Sampling**: Each member has an equal, known probability of selection; 384 responses yield 95% confidence with ±5% margin of error.
- **Stratified Sampling**: Dividing the population into subgroups (strata) and sampling separately from each, ensuring adequate representation of minority subgroups.
- **Nonprobabilistic Approaches**: Including convenience sampling, snowball sampling (respondents recruit others), self-selected surveys, intercept sampling (surveying every Nth user), and oversampling (collecting large numbers of responses to compensate for lack of random selection).

**Question Design Framework**:
- **Open-ended questions**: Allow flexible responses but are harder to analyze and require careful wording to elicit useful information.
- **Closed-ended questions with ordered responses**: Likert scales and similar ranked options; respondents select one position on a continuum.
- **Closed-ended questions with unordered responses**: Categorical choices (e.g., software types, demographic categories); may allow multiple selections.
- **Common pitfalls**: Double-barreled questions (asking two things at once), negative wording, biased framing, and "hot-button" words that trigger emotional responses.

**Survey Structure Principles**:
- Begin with clear instructions and navigational guidance
- Group related questions together to reduce cognitive load
- Place engaging questions first, demographic questions last
- Use contingent questions (with clear skip logic) for conditional paths
- Maintain adequate white space and readable formatting

**Pilot Testing Framework**: A three-stage process: (1) expert review by knowledgeable colleagues, (2) cognitive interviews with potential respondents, and (3) a full pilot study with representative users completing the entire survey.

**Validity and Reliability Assessment**:
- Exploratory factor analysis to identify which questions are most predictive (factor loadings >0.6 retained)
- Cronbach's alpha coefficient for measuring internal reliability
- Demographic data checks to assess representativeness

## Important Takeaways

1. **Surveys are powerful but frequently misused**: They excel at collecting "shallow" data from large populations quickly and cost-effectively, but are inappropriate for deep, detailed investigations. They work best when combined with complementary methods like interviews, diary studies, or usability testing.

2. **HCI research legitimately uses nonprobabilistic sampling**: Unlike sociology and public policy communities that demand strict random sampling, HCI researchers regularly collect their own data and use convenience samples — this is considered valid within the community, provided demographic data and response volumes support the findings.

3. **Question wording is a minefield**: Poorly worded questions (double-barreled, negatively phrased, biased) can invalidate an entire survey. Pilot testing is not optional — it is essential for identifying confusing, misleading, or ambiguous questions before data collection begins.

4. **Survey format should match the research context**: Paper, email, and web-based surveys each have trade-offs in terms of accessibility, cost, response speed, data entry accuracy, and anonymity. Web-based surveys are now most common, but researchers must ensure digital accessibility and consider populations without internet access.

5. **Response rate requires active management**: Simply distributing a survey is insufficient. Researchers should use precontact letters, establish credibility through trusted authorities, provide multiple reminders through varied channels, and make the return process as easy as possible.

6. **Existing validated survey tools should be reused**: Instruments like CSUQ, QUIS, SUMI, and WAMMI have been tested and validated — there is no need to reinvent the wheel when existing tools address the research question.
