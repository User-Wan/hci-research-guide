# Chapter 6 Summary: Interviews and Focus Groups (Diaries)

## Main Topic

This chapter provides an in-depth treatment of diary studies as a research method in human-computer interaction (HCI). It explains how diaries — regular recordings of events made at the time they occur — fill critical gaps between controlled laboratory experiments, naturalistic observation, and retrospective surveys. The chapter covers the rationale for diary research, different diary types, participant selection, data collection methods, and analysis procedures.

## Key Concepts Introduced

- **Diary as a Research Method**: A diary is a document where individuals record events as they happen, including activities, explanations, and personal reflections. Unlike surveys that rely on recall, diaries minimize recall bias by capturing data in real time, making them ideal for fluid data such as mood, perception, and temporal patterns.

- **Time Diaries vs. General Diaries**: Time diaries specifically focus on how individuals allocate time across activities, requiring regular temporal entries. General diaries may be recorded on an infrequent or non-temporal basis. Time diaries are particularly prevalent in HCI because many research questions involve how long users spend on tasks or how time is lost to frustration.

- **Feedback vs. Elicitation Diaries**: A fundamental typology in diary research. In a **feedback diary**, the diary data itself is the complete data set — the diary IS the data collection method. In an **elicitation diary**, participants record brief entries (photos, short text, audio) that serve as prompts for deeper exploration during follow-up interviews. Feedback diaries emphasize events the researcher defines; elicitation diaries emphasize events meaningful to the user.

- **Ecological Validity**: Diaries excel at capturing data in naturalistic settings — across multiple technologies, locations, and environments — where controlled studies or observation would be impractical or would alter behavior. This makes them particularly valuable for studying mobile device usage, on-the-go activities, and cross-platform behaviors.

## Main Methods and Frameworks

**When to Use Diaries**:
- When little prior research exists and hypotheses cannot yet be formulated
- When technology use spans multiple devices, locations, and contexts
- When the data of interest cannot be observed or measured experimentally (e.g., user intent, emotional states, frustration)
- When observer presence would alter behavior
- When used in triangulation with other methods (lab studies, interviews, surveys) to strengthen findings

**Participant Selection**:
- Unlike surveys, diary studies do not require large random samples; quality of insight matters more than quantity
- Snowball sampling is common — initial participants recruit others from their networks
- Participants must possess three qualities: understanding of the study's purpose, motivation to maintain regular records, and competence with the technology being studied
- Typical diary studies involve 10–25 participants recording entries over 1–2 weeks

**Feedback Diary Design Decisions**:
- **Trigger events**: What prompts a diary entry — occurrence of a specific event, a set time each day, random interruptions, or crossing a threshold?
- **Structure level**: Ranges from highly structured (Likert scales, checkboxes, timestamps, predefined categories) to highly unstructured (open reflection, blog-like entries)
- **Recording format**: Paper, electronic spreadsheets, web forms, voicemail, smartphone apps, or smartwatch-triggered prompts

**Elicitation Diary Design**:
- Participants capture minimal data points — photographs, short audio clips, brief text messages
- These lightweight entries are later used as prompts during semi-structured interviews
- Example: 22 users photographed paper documents they wanted to digitize over 7 days, producing 381 entries that were expanded upon in interviews

**Data Collection Media**:
- Should match what is most natural in participants' everyday lives
- Electronic devices (smartphones, tablets) are increasingly preferred because they are unobtrusive, allow real-time entry, and can provide reminders
- Voicemail has been used effectively for mobile studies where the diary subject (mobile phone) might otherwise conflict with the recording method
- Smartwatches can detect activity patterns (e.g., hand-to-mouth eating gestures) and prompt diary entries automatically

**Participant Guidance**:
- Provide clear definitions of terminology (what constitutes a "crash," "interruption," etc.)
- Define precisely when entries should be made
- Offer regular feedback on participation (but not on content, to avoid bias)
- Do NOT link payment to number of entries, as this incentivizes inflated reporting
- Payment should be for overall participation, not per-entry

**Data Analysis**:
- Quantitative data (timestamps, counts, Likert ratings) can be analyzed with standard statistical methods
- Qualitative data (descriptive text) requires content analysis — developing coding categories and coding text according to meaning
- Coding schemes should be validated between multiple researchers
- Mixed-method analysis (combining quantitative and qualitative) is common

## Important Takeaways

1. **Diaries capture what other methods cannot**: They record user-defined events (intended but unperformed actions, emotional states, frustration experiences) that neither observation nor automated data collection can detect. One study found that 30–43% of computer time was lost to frustrating experiences — a finding only possible through time diary methodology.

2. **The feedback/elicitation distinction is crucial**: Feedback diaries yield complete, self-contained data sets, while elicitation diaries are designed as springboards for interview-based deep exploration. The choice between them shapes the entire study design, from what participants record to how data is analyzed.

3. **Ecological validity is the primary advantage**: Diaries study technology use where it actually happens — across devices, locations, and contexts — rather than in artificial lab settings. They are the method of choice for studying mobile, cross-platform, and on-the-go behaviors.

4. **Diary studies require careful participant management**: Motivation tends to decline over time, making 1–2 weeks the practical maximum duration. Regular (but not content-specific) reminders help sustain participation. Participants must be intrinsically motivated by understanding the study's importance, not incentivized by per-entry payments that would corrupt data quality.

5. **Diaries work best in combination with other methods**: When paired with interviews, surveys, or lab studies, diaries compensate for the weaknesses of each complementary method. The FETCH system study exemplifies this — lab testing first, then diary study, then interviews — with each phase building on previous findings.

6. **Recall bias is the enemy diaries are designed to defeat**: Surveys asking users to remember past moods, feelings, or temporal patterns produce unreliable data due to personality-driven recall distortion and simple forgetting. Diaries minimize the gap between event occurrence and recording, preserving data fidelity.
